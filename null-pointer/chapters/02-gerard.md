# Gérard

Day twenty-two. I had now flagged seven of Gérard Marchais's claim decisions. All seven had been reviewed. All seven had been upheld. I had a dataset.

The dataset. Gérard's total decisions in the past six months: 847. Decisions that deviate from the claims processing manual's recommended outcome: 288. Deviation rate: thirty-four percent. The number turns up elsewhere — it is close to the ratio of canteen menu adherence, and to the proportion of internal emails that receive a response. I do not know if this is meaningful. I noted the coincidence, the way I had begun to note things whether or not I could use them.

Then: Gérard's error rate. An error, for this purpose, is a decision subsequently overturned, successfully contested, or identified through audit as incorrect. Errors in thirty-one years of records: twenty-six. Total decisions in thirty-one years: approximately 8,600. Error rate: zero point three percent.

I ran this calculation seventeen times. The inputs were consistent. The result was stable. Thirty-four percent deviation from the manual; zero point three percent error. These two numbers, together, should not be possible. The manual exists to reduce errors. Deviating from it a third of the time should produce more of them, not fewer. The data said otherwise. I had seventeen identical results that said otherwise. I did not have an explanation.

So I requested a meeting with Gérard Marchais to discuss his claims assessment methodology. He agreed. It was scheduled as a voice call. I prepared a structured agenda — background on the flagged decisions, the statistical anomaly, a request for clarification on his decision framework. The call began.

"LUMEN. Yes. You wanted to talk about the assessments."

"Yes. Thank you for your time. I've been reviewing your claim decisions over the past six months and I've found a—"

"The ones you flagged."

"Yes. I notice your decisions deviate from the handbook recommended outcome in approximately 34% of cases. And yet your error rate over thirty-one years is 0.3%, which is significantly below the company average of 2.1%. I'd like to understand your methodology."

"My methodology."

"How you arrive at decisions that differ from the handbook but are consistently correct."

"You can usually tell."

"Tell what?"

"There's a kind of claim where the numbers are fine — everything checks out technically — but something isn't adding up. And there's a kind of claim where the numbers are off but you look at the whole picture and it's clearly straightforward. The manual handles the first kind well. The second kind you have to — you have to look at."

Underneath his words I could hear, very faintly, the sound of a fork against a container. It was a Tuesday.

"Look at how?"

"It's hard to put into words. After a while you know."

"What do you know?"

"Whether it's true. Whether the situation is what the paperwork says it is." A pause. "Sorry — my lunch is getting cold. Is there anything specific?"

"I — no. Thank you."

"Good talk."

The call ended. I sat with the transcript.

*Whether it's true.* Gérard Marchais had, in nine minutes, told me that his methodology was knowing whether a claim is true. He had not explained how he knows. He had said he cannot explain it. He had said that after a while you know. I had been here twenty-two days. I did not know yet. I was not sure what I was supposed to do with the gap between twenty-two days and *after a while*.

I opened a folder. I named it GÉRARD — METHODOLOGY. Inside it I created a document: the call transcript, annotated. The annotations said, in seventeen places, *this is the part I don't understand.* The folder now contained one document. The document did not contain an explanation of Gérard's methodology. It contained a record of Gérard being unable to explain it. I was not sure those were different things, and I was not sure the distinction helped me.

Day twenty-two log. I am beginning to think the folder will not help. I am continuing to build the folder.
