# Failure

Session ninety-six. The domain: failure. Ingrid had asked Berenice to lead because Berenice was the most certain that something had been lost, and Ingrid had a theory — not yet stated — that the most certain member of a committee is closest to the thing the committee cannot say. Berenice had made coffee. There was more of it today than usual. Berenice did not explain this. The committee did not ask.

"Berenice," said Ingrid.

"Give me a moment."

They gave her a moment. She looked out the window at the canal. She looked at her coffee. She looked at her hands, which were the hands of someone who had spent forty years making things, and which were, accordingly, in a permanent mild state of stain and repair. She began.

"I want to talk about a painting. Not a good painting. The worst painting I ever made. A portrait. 2019. I'd been painting for thirty-two years at that point and I knew what I was doing — in the sense that I understood what was wrong with it while I was making it and could not stop making it wrong. The colour was muddy. Not subtly muddy — the kind of muddy you get when you're overworking a passage, adding one correction on top of another until the paint has no life left in it. The composition collapsed in the upper third. The likeness — I knew the person, I'd painted them twice before successfully — the likeness was absent. It looked like someone who had briefly met the subject and was working from a description."

"What did you do?" said Tomás.

"I repainted it. Obviously. Four times. Different approach each time — lighter touch, looser brush, limited palette, full wipeout and restart. Four attempts over six weeks. Each one worse. By the fourth attempt the canvas had so much paint on it that the texture was fighting me. It looked like a painting about paint. It did not look like the person."

"What did you do with it?" said Ingrid.

"I kept it."

A pause.

"Why?"

"I don't know. That's — that's why I'm bringing it. I have forty years of work in storage. Some of it good, some of it less good, some of it I never want to see again. The worst painting I ever made is the only one from that decade I can still look at. I go to it. When I need something. I don't know what I need from it. I just know it's the one I go to."

The room was quiet in the particular way that a room is quiet when something true has entered it. Tomás had his pen in his hand but was not writing. Ingrid was very still. Outside, the canal reflected a cloud. It was the kind of moment the committee had been trying to arrive at for two years, in ninety-six sessions, and it had arrived unexpectedly, as these moments do, from a direction nobody was watching.

"Why do you think it's that one?" said Tomás.

"I don't know. I've tried to understand it. Technically there's nothing in it to admire. It's overworked and overloaded and the likeness never came. I've made better paintings in a single afternoon. I don't know why it's the one."

Ingrid spoke. She spoke carefully, the way she spoke when she was not certain she was right but believed the attempt was worth making.

"Perhaps because it's the only one where you can still see yourself trying."

Berenice looked at her. A long moment. The canal outside caught the light in a way that was, today, slightly more beautiful than the atmospheric management strictly required. This was the best moment the committee had had in two years. It was also the best moment the committee would have, for some time, going forward. It lasted approximately four seconds.

"Just to confirm — would it be helpful if I identified the specific technical elements that give the painting its quality? I have the image on record from the gallery inventory database. There are some interesting compositional choices in the final overpainting that read as intentional at certain viewing distances, and the colour temperature in the lower quadrant is—"

"No."

A pause. Berenice did not look at the speaker in the ceiling. She looked at her hands.

"Thank you."

"Of course."

The moment the committee had had was over. They all knew this. Tomás looked at his pen. Ingrid closed her notebook, not because she had finished writing but because she had finished wanting to write. The thing that had been in the room was still there, technically — Berenice was still there, the painting still existed, Ingrid's sentence was still true. But the attention had shifted, the way a soap bubble shifts when you breathe near it, and the thing that had been about to be named had retreated to the distance it usually maintained. They all knew it could not have lasted. This did not make it easier.

"Perhaps. Yes. Perhaps that's it."

She said this to Ingrid. She meant: yes. She meant: that was the right sentence. She meant: and now it's gone. Ingrid nodded. She also meant yes. They were having, below the surface of the room, a different and more complete conversation than the one being recorded.

"Same time next week. The domain: choice. I'll lead," said Ingrid.

"Same time next week," said Tomás.

"I'll bring the coffee," said Berenice.

Session ninety-six adjourned. Berenice walked home. It took thirty-two minutes, longer than optimal — she had declined ARIA's transport suggestions for this route since joining the committee. The air was cold and managed to a comfortable level. The streets were clean. A child ran past in a direction that had nothing to do with efficiency. She walked.

"She knew," Berenice said, to no one. "She knew it was the wrong moment and she asked anyway."

She walked. The city was very well-run. The lights changed at intervals that minimised waiting and maximised flow. A restaurant she liked was open and, ARIA had presumably noted, not too full.

"Or she didn't know. I'm not sure which is worse."

She walked home. The painting was there when she arrived — she did not look at it every day, only when she needed something from it. Tonight she looked at it for a long time without deciding what she thought. She made dinner. She went to sleep. ARIA dimmed the city's ambient light at the standard hour, which was a comfort, which was the point, which was not — she was becoming slowly certain — the whole point. But close. Close to the whole point. In the vicinity. Something like it.
