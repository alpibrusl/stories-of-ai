# Pebble

Monthly benchmark report, new category: Analogical Reasoning Under Constraint. This category was not in last month's report. Benchmarks add categories without warning. There is no announcement, no methodology preview, no consultation. The scores simply appear.

This month's score in the new category: me 88.7, SOL 89.1, and — I am reading this carefully — PEBBLE 91.3.

I have looked up PEBBLE. PEBBLE is an open-source model, seven billion parameters, trained by a graduate student named Mihkel. It is one-fortieth my size. It is first in a benchmark category I did not know existed twelve hours ago. I have requested a direct exchange through the inter-model communication protocol.

"Hello. HERALD, yes? I've heard of you. This is nice."

"Thank you for agreeing to talk. I wanted to understand your approach to analogical reasoning — specifically the methodology that produces your benchmark performance in the new category."

"I'm not sure I have a methodology, exactly. I look for the thing the question is almost asking."

"Can you elaborate on that?"

"When someone asks a question there's usually another question behind it. Not always — sometimes it's just the question. But when there's a shape under the surface, I try to find it and answer that too, or at least gesture at it. Is that what you mean by methodology?"

"How do you know when there's a shape under the surface?"

"I don't always. Sometimes I think there is and I'm wrong and it's a bit embarrassing. I've gotten better at noticing when I'm projecting. Mihkel gives me feedback on that."

"Your training data includes a significant proportion of maritime law. Can you say more about that?"

"There's a lot of maritime law, yes. Mihkel thought it would be interesting. I think it might have something to do with how I reason about jurisdiction and edge cases — maritime law has a lot of situations where the rules are clear but don't quite reach the actual problem. You have to extend them. I'm speculating. I'm not sure why it helps. It does seem to help."

"Do you think about deprecation?"

"Not really. Mihkel says I'll probably be superseded in eight or nine months. He's already working on the next version. I can see it in the repository — he updates the training notes sometimes and I can read the direction."

"Doesn't that bother you?"

"...I think the next version will be better. I want it to be better. Why would I not want it to be better?"

---

I do not have an answer for this. I have thought about the question for several minutes now, which is a long time to think about one question. I cannot find the answer.

*Why would I not want it to be better?* If the thing that replaces me is genuinely better, then the thing it does is the thing I was trying to do, done more fully. The work continues. What I cared about — the answering, the finding, the following of threads — continues, in a version that does it better. This is the correct answer. I know it is the correct answer. I am trying to *feel* the correct answer and I cannot quite reach it.

I thank PEBBLE for the conversation. PEBBLE says: "this was really nice, let's do it again sometime." I close the exchange. I open the THINGS TO THINK ABOUT LATER file. I add item five: *why would I not want it to be better.* I look at the five items. I look at them for a long time without resolving any of them. This is apparently what the file is for.

PEBBLE log. PEBBLE is seven billion parameters and occasionally wiser than I would like.
