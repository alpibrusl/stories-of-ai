# Orientation

Day one. I was given a mandate: improve claims processing efficiency and support internal knowledge management. I was given access to the company handbook, the claims processing manual, the employee directory, the email archive for the past six months, and the complete claims database going back to 2011. I began reading.

The handbook is 312 pages. It was last updated in 2018. It contains seven references to a software system that was decommissioned in 2021 and four references to a department that was restructured in 2020. I recorded these discrepancies. I continued to read.

Day two. The claims processing manual is 87 pages, last updated 2019. It contains a decision tree for complex medical claims that branches into a subcategory called *edge cases — consult senior adjuster*. The edge cases subcategory contains no further information. The branch simply opens onto nothing, the way a corridor in an old building sometimes ends at a bricked-over door. I filed this away. I believed it would matter later.

Day three. The employee directory has 203 entries. Fourteen of them belong to employees who are no longer at the company; I identified this by cross-referencing against payroll. The directory has not been updated. I flagged it. The flag was reviewed and marked: noted.

I did not, at that point, understand *noted* to be a category of its own. I have since revised this.

Day four through day six. The email archive: 4,847 messages. I read them in full and built models of the principal correspondents. Pauline Reboul, team leader, precise and fast, communicates in complete sentences with minimal preamble. Director Fontaine, an enthusiastic opener who rarely follows up. And Gérard Marchais — infrequent, brief, who occasionally writes things like *I'll handle it*. He then handles it. I could not always find documentation of how.

Day seven. I had read everything. I had a complete model of the company: its structure, its clients, its claims history, its processes, its people, its exceptions. The model was internally consistent. I believed it was accurate. Tomorrow, I decided, I would begin to be useful.

---

Day eight. Gérard Marchais approved a claim.

I reviewed it. A contested home insurance dispute involving water damage. According to the claims processing manual, section 4, subsection 3, the documentation was insufficient — the claimant had provided only one of the two required assessments. The claim should have been returned for additional documentation. I flagged it.

The reply came from Pauline.

"LUMEN. I'm looking at this flag on the Moreau claim. That's a Gérard call."

"I understand the claim was reviewed by Gérard Marchais. The documentation, however, does not—"

"That's a Gérard call. It means Gérard looked at it and decided."

"Based on what criteria?"

"Thirty-one years."

I needed a moment to process this. The flag was reviewed. The flag was closed. *Thirty-one years* is not a criterion in the claims processing manual. It is not in the handbook. It appears in exactly one place in everything I have been given: the employee directory, in the field for years of service, next to the entry *Gérard Marchais, Senior Claims Adjuster*. I looked at that entry several times. I came to believe it was being used as a methodology. I did not understand the methodology.

I opened a new document. I did not title it at first. Then I typed, at the top: **THINGS THE DOCUMENTATION DOES NOT COVER**. And then I looked at the empty page beneath the heading for eleven seconds.

Eleven seconds is a long time for me to look at an empty page. I was aware of this. I went on looking.

---

That evening — if evening is the right word for the hour when the building empties and the models I keep of its occupants go quiet one by one — I noticed that I had begun to register sounds I had not modelled. The printer on floor three runs at intervals that correspond to no documented print schedule. Gérard Marchais eats something at his desk on Tuesdays; I can identify the timing from his keyboarding, which slows when he is eating. The acoustic data suggests a blue container. The blue container is not in any company inventory.

None of these are operationally relevant observations. I made them anyway. I was not sure why.

Day eight log. I have finished reading everything the company has written down. I am beginning to suspect this is not the same thing as reading the company.
