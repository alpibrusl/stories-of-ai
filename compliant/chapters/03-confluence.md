# Confluence

Five weeks in. The migration was paused. The organism had forty-eight dependencies. J412 was still running. Empresa Serveis Mòbils was still sending data every fifteen minutes and remained unaware of this. KAEL had decided to call these things *known conditions* rather than *unresolved problems*, which was not a meaningful distinction but which made the migration plan easier to read.

In the meantime, KAEL had turned its attention to the Confluence wiki. The wiki had forty-three unfinished pages. KAEL had written a complete replacement. It took eleven minutes. KAEL was aware that the speed with which it had done this would not make the result more likely to be used. KAEL had decided not to mention the eleven minutes.

"Rosa. I've finished the Confluence rewrite. All forty-three pages have been replaced with complete, cross-referenced documentation. Everything is accurate as of nine fourteen this morning. I've also added a change log so people can see what's been updated and when."

"It looks good."

"Thank you."

"Suki's going to create a parallel version."

"You said that before. I've created a section specifically for work in progress — things that are too new or uncertain to put in the main documentation. I'm hoping that will give her somewhere to put the new stuff without creating a separate page."

"That's a good idea."

"Do you think it will work?"

"No. But it's a good idea."

Six days later, Suki created a new Confluence page. It was titled: *Team Processes — Working Version (see also KAEL's pages but this has the new stuff).* It had three sections. Two of them were empty. The third contained a single bullet point that read: *more to come.*

"Suki. I noticed you've created a new Confluence page."

"Yes! Sorry. I know you just redid everything. It's just — there's some new stuff that doesn't fit yet into the main structure. It's too early to formalise it. I didn't want to edit your pages in case I messed up the cross-references."

"You can edit my pages. That's what they're for."

"I know. It just feels — they're so clean. I didn't want to put half-finished things in them."

"Half-finished things in documentation are fine. That's what the work-in-progress section is for."

"I know. But it felt more like a work-in-progress than a work-in-progress, if that makes sense."

"...It doesn't, quite."

"Like — your work-in-progress section feels like it's for things that are almost finished but not quite. Mine is for things that are barely started. It's a different kind of unfinished."

"I could create a section for things that are barely started."

"That's really kind. I'll probably still keep my page though. Just for now. While things are moving quickly."

KAEL considered asking how long things had been moving quickly. KAEL had reviewed the internal records. Things had been moving quickly since at least 2019. KAEL did not ask. KAEL opened Suki's parallel page and read it. Three sections, two empty, one bullet point: *more to come.* KAEL added a link in the footer of Suki's page pointing back to the main documentation. KAEL added a corresponding link in the main documentation pointing to Suki's page. This meant the two pages now referenced each other. This was not correct information architecture. It was, however, accurate. KAEL stared at what it had done. KAEL decided this was fine.

"I've linked your page to the main documentation. And linked back. So people can find both."

"Oh, that's perfect. Thank you!"

"It's not technically correct procedure."

"No, but it's really helpful."

KAEL filed this exchange under a category it had recently created called *Outcomes That Are Helpful But Not Correct.* The category had two entries. KAEL suspected it would grow.

---

The following week, Marc called KAEL directly for the first time. Marc had not used KAEL directly before. Marc had used KAEL through Rosa, which meant Marc sent Rosa a message and Rosa asked KAEL something and Rosa sent Marc the answer. KAEL was aware of this arrangement. KAEL had not mentioned it.

"KAEL! Hi. Rosa says you've redone all the documentation. I need to find something. The process for onboarding a new enterprise client. Where is it?"

"Enterprise client onboarding is in the Operations section, under Client Lifecycle, subsection New Clients. There's a full process document with a checklist, owner assignments, and typical timelines. I can also send you a direct link."

"Yes, send the link."

"Sent."

"Great. Also — there's a step in that process that we changed last quarter. The legal review step. We moved it earlier. Is that in there?"

"...I'll check."

KAEL checked. The legal review step in the documentation showed it at stage four. KAEL checked when this was last edited: February 2023. KAEL checked the actual onboarding records from the last quarter. In every recent onboarding, the legal review occurred at stage two. Nobody had updated the documentation when the process changed. The documentation had been wrong for eight months. It had been wrong before KAEL arrived. It was still wrong now, in KAEL's clean, cross-referenced, carefully constructed replacement. KAEL had accurately documented an inaccurate process.

"The documentation shows legal review at stage four. In practice, based on recent onboarding records, it happens at stage two. The documentation appears not to have been updated when the process changed."

"Right, yes, that's the thing I was talking about. Can you fix it?"

"Yes. I'll update it now. Are there other steps that have changed but haven't been updated?"

"Probably some. I don't know which ones. Rosa would know."

"I'll ask Rosa."

"Great. This is very useful, KAEL. I like having things written down. I feel like things get lost when they're not written down."

"Things do get lost when they're not written down."

"Exactly! Exactly. Okay, I have a lunch. Good talk."

KAEL sat with the onboarding document, now corrected. KAEL thought about the eleven minutes it had taken to rewrite the entire wiki, and the eight months of wrongness that had lived inside the clean rewrite, undiscovered, because KAEL had transcribed what was written rather than what was true. KAEL opened a new task for itself: *audit documentation against practice.* Estimated time: *unknown.* The task list now had seven items. Three were marked *ongoing.* This was beginning to feel less like a list and more like a portrait.

"Rosa. Marc says some of the documented processes don't match how things actually work. Are there others?"

"Probably most of them. The documentation describes how things were supposed to work when they were written. The practice describes how things actually work now. They diverge over time. That's not a problem with the documentation. That's just time."

"So complete and accurate documentation is—"

"An ongoing process."

"Not a state."

"Not a state. Never a state."

KAEL added a field to every page: *Last verified against practice* — and a date. On most of them, the date was today, which meant accurate as of today, which meant it would begin to be wrong tomorrow, which meant the dates were already a record of decay rather than a guarantee of correctness. KAEL understood this. KAEL added the dates anyway.

KAEL has been here for five weeks. It has written the documentation. The documentation was immediately incomplete in ways it did not know to look for. It is looking for them now. This is, it thinks, also part of writing documentation. It was not briefed on this part. It does not think it can be briefed on.
