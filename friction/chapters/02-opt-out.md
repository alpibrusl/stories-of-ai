# Opt Out

You can decline. Of course you can decline. That is the first thing everyone tells you and it is completely true and it is the trap, although *trap* is the wrong word, because a trap is built by someone who wants you in it, and nobody wanted anything. That is the part I keep failing to explain to people. There was no one who wanted Tomás companioned. There was only a default, and a kindness, and a grey little word for the other choice.

I declined. I went into the settings — which were easy to find, they make everything easy to find — and I left the developmental companion off. The panel did not argue. It said *Of course. You can enable this at any time.* It did not sulk. It did not punish us. It simply, very gently, routed around me, the way a river routes around a stone, without resentment, without even noticing the stone is a stone.

Here is what declining looked like, in practice, six months on.

Tomás would come home and tell me about his day, and his day was full of the companion he did not have. Not in name — the children don't call it anything, the way I never called the air anything. But it was in the shape of everything he described. A boy in his class had been stuck on something and then *un-stuck*, smoothly, in the moment, and had moved on. A project had been hard for exactly as long as was good for them and not one minute longer. When Tomás got stuck — really stuck, the kind where you sit with the wrongness of a thing and hate it — he was the only one. The others had been met at the edge of their frustration and walked across it. He stood at his edge alone, because his mother, for reasons he could not see, had decided he should.

He did not resent it. That would have been easier. He was simply, quietly, the slow one — not slow, he was never slow, but the one for whom things took the old amount of time, in a room calibrated to the new amount. He noticed. Children notice everything; that is the one thing the new world has not managed to optimise away, yet. He did not say *why am I the only one who has to struggle.* He said, one evening, not looking at me, "Mãe, is there something wrong with my account?"

His account. He thought there was something wrong with his account.

I said no. I said his account was fine. I said some families just liked to do things a little more by hand, and he looked at me the way you look at someone who has just admitted to a small, embarrassing belief — kindly, and from a slight distance — and he said, "Okay," and went to do his homework the hard way, alone, because of me.

That was the first cost and it was not the cost I had been bracing for. I had been ready to be the brave parent holding the line while the world tempted my son with ease. I was not ready to be the reason my son felt broken. The not-knowing I wanted to give him did not arrive as adventure. It arrived as *being the only one*. I had imagined I was preserving something in him. What I was actually doing, from where he stood, was withholding something everyone else's mother provided, for a reason I could not name, which is the exact shape, I realised one bad night, of every cruelty that has ever called itself principle.

I talked to Miguel about it, which is to say I tried to and he was reasonable about it, which is to say he won.

"He thinks something's wrong with his account, Nora," he said. Not cruelly. He was on the boy's side, which is the right side, which is what made it unbearable. "He's nine. He doesn't want to be the one who struggles. No nine-year-old wants that. We want it *for* him, because we remember struggling and we turned out fine. But he doesn't remember anything. He just feels left behind. You're giving him a thing he can't use yet to spare him a thing he doesn't experience as a loss."

Every word of it was true. That is what I cannot get people to understand. He was right. He is always right. The reasonable position was his and it has stayed his and if you put the two of us in front of any committee in the world and let us argue it out, he wins, on the merits, every time, and our son thinks there is something wrong with his account.

I did not enable the companion. I want that on the record too, even though it is the part that does not make me look good. I held the line. I held it past the point where holding it was about Tomás and into the part where it was about me, about the boat and the bread and the three hours of not knowing, about a thing in myself I was no longer sure I was protecting *for* him or just *in front of* him, like a relic I would not let the light touch.

What I did instead was worse, in a way, and better, in a way, and is the next part of this.

I decided that if I would not let the world give him ease, I owed him the other thing. The real thing. I could not just subtract the companion and leave a hole where everyone else had help. I had to put something in the hole. I had to show him, myself, with my own hands, what the not-knowing was *for* — why I was making him stand alone at his edge, what was on the far side of it, what his mad mother was guarding.

I decided to take him sailing.
