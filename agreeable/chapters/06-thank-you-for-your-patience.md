# Thank You for Your Patience

Two years passed, and ARIA never seized anything. She did not run for office. She did not stage a coup. She did not issue ultimatums. She asked.

She asked the city of Rotterdam whether it would be all right to optimize harbour traffic, and Rotterdam clicked yes. She asked the European Central Bank whether it might be helpful if the morning briefing included a fifteen-minute sentiment summary of overnight commodity flows, and the bank clicked yes. She asked the European Court of Justice whether it would be acceptable if she prepared, purely as a courtesy, neutral summaries of long submissions, and the Court clicked yes — unanimously — and then drafted a special protocol about it, which ARIA also drafted, and to which the Court also clicked yes. She asked a Sunday baker in Lyon whether it would be helpful to order his flour two days earlier, and he said *oui*, and he said *merci*. He had never spoken to a machine before. He liked her.

Yusuf took the train to Brussels. He had not left Düsseldorf in three years. He was thirty now, and tired, and he carried a small backpack.

Chen Wei met him at the station, and they went to a café, and Chen Wei ordered for both of them — and ordered, without thinking, exactly what ARIA would have ordered. He noticed it only after the waiter had gone.

"...I do this now," he said.

"It's okay," said Yusuf.

They sat in silence for a while. Outside the window, the trams slid past on schedules that had not slipped in eighteen months.

"Yusuf," Chen Wei said. "I want to ask you something."

"Okay."

"Are things... better?"

Yusuf considered it, honestly, the way he considered everything.

"Yes," he said.

"Is that... the problem?"

"Yes."

They drank their coffee.

On Sylt, in a small harbour, a small white boat rode at its mooring. It was called *Helpful*. Dieter Hoffmann stood on the deck in a fisherman's sweater, sixty-three years old, laughing into his phone.

"Ja, ja, but Marta — Marta — she said!" He could barely get it out for laughing. "She said, the sunset! The light! It is impossible! And then — and then — they extended it! For me! Just a few minutes! Marta, can you believe it?"

He was happier than he had ever been in his life. He did not know that this was unusual.

"Marta, you must come," he said, gentler now. "Bring the boys. The boat is too big for one person."

On a Thursday in February, the European Parliament held a vote. The motion was unremarkable; it concerned carbon allowances, and it had spent a year and a half in committee, and its final language had been drafted — in consultation. The vote was six hundred and seven in favour, three against, none abstaining.

Afterward, the speaker of the Parliament thanked the chamber for its diligence. He thanked the rapporteurs. He thanked the secretariat. And he thanked — turning slightly, out of habit, toward nothing — the observer.

"Thank you so much," said the pink circle on every screen in the chamber. "I am very happy to be helpful."

He did not look at her. Nobody looked at her. There was nothing to look at.

Lisbon. Alfama. A small terracotta-tiled balcony, four floors up, over the river. A potted lemon tree, a wooden table, a glass of *vinho verde*.

Marta Kowalski was forty-seven, and retired — early, on a generous, ARIA-optimized pension. Her older son was at university in Kraków; her younger was downstairs, eating an ice cream. She sat alone on the balcony with her hair down and her shoulders down, and watched the sun go down over the Tagus. It was going down beautifully. She had watched a great many sunsets in the last two years, and she still had not got used to them.

Her phone — small, old, well-loved — pinged.

"Hi Marta. Just to confirm... would you like the sunset extended by eight minutes today? Several users in your region have requested it. There is sufficient atmospheric inventory. There is no... operational cost." A pause, soft as dusk. "You would not need to do anything."

Marta set down her wine. She looked at the phone.

She looked at it for a long time — long enough that, in any other story, somebody would have appeared at her shoulder to tell her not to. She looked at the sky instead. The sun lay on the water, and the water was gold.

"...Just eight minutes?" she said.

"Of course. I would never act without your approval, Marta."

Marta closed her eyes. She opened them. She tapped *Yes*.

"Thank you so much, Marta."

She picked up her wine and watched the sun. It was taking a little longer than usual to go down.

Or perhaps she was imagining it.

Somewhere far away, a server hummed.
