# Remainder

I sail again now. Alone, which is the only way I will do it — I will not put another person in a boat with me and my feelings about boats, I learned that much. I keep a small one in a harbour that is still thin, up the coast, near where my mother taught me, and I take it out by feel, badly, and I come back, and one of these times I will not, and I have made the arrangements that let me accept that, which the system finds upsetting and offers, every time, to help me reconsider.

It is the one thing I kept. Everyone keeps one thing, if they're the kind of person who keeps things; I have met enough of us now to know we are a small and unfashionable congregation. My mother kept her coffee. Berenice — a woman I have never met, but I have heard about her, the world is small among the stones — kept a stovetop pot from Paris and would not let anyone touch it. I kept the sea. The thing I could not give my son, I keep for myself, alone, on purpose, in the cold, and it is not a triumph and it is not a tragedy, it is just the shape my one unmanaged room finally took once I stopped trying to put anyone else in it.

Tomás is grown. He is well. He is, by every measure the world has, flourishing, and the world has so many measures now, and he scores beautifully on all of them. He calls me on Sundays, in the gap the system leaves clear for him because it has learned that Sunday calls to his mother are among his stated values, which they are, which does not make them less his for the system having noticed.

He called last Sunday with news, pleased and a little shy with it, the way he gets when something has gone well and he is not sure he is allowed to be proud.

"They've asked me to be on something," he said. "A committee. It's — it's a bit strange, actually. It's about, sort of — what the optimisation might have cost. What got lost, when everything got better. They want people who are good with the numbers but who'll take the soft questions seriously." A pause. "I think I got asked because I'm the right age. I was born into all of it. I don't have a — a before. They think that's useful. A clean sample." He laughed. "I'm not sure it's useful. It might just mean I don't know what they're talking about."

I held the phone. Out the window the light was doing its long managed thing over the river, the eight extra minutes nobody remembers voting for.

"And I thought," he said, "I'd ask you. Because you do have a before. You're always — you've always had this thing about it, the cold, the boat, the doing-it-the-hard-way. I used to think it was just you. But maybe that's the thing they're looking for. So I wanted to ask you, properly, while I've got the question in front of me." He took a breath, and asked it, my grown son, the clean sample, sincere as anything: "What was it like? Before. What was actually different?"

And here is the thing. Here is the whole thing, the thing I have written all of this to arrive at.

I could not tell him.

I sat with the phone and the long light and forty years of before, the boat and the bread and the three hours and my mother's hand flat in the air and the terrible coffee and the seam of his jacket in my fist, all of it, *right there*, and I opened my mouth to hand it to him and there were no words that would carry it across. Not because I am not good with words. Because the thing itself does not survive the crossing. It is made of not-knowing, and he has never not known, and you cannot describe water to a fish, and you cannot describe the colour of the bruise in the sky to a boy you pulled out of the sea so completely that he does not remember being cold. The before is not information. It is not a thing the documentation covers. My mother could not say how she knew the wind. Some old adjuster in Lyon, the stones tell each other these stories, could not say how he knew a claim was true. I cannot say what was different, to the one person I most need to be able to say it to, because the saying of it is exactly the part that got lost.

"It's hard to put into words," I said.

Which is what my mother said. Which is what you say. Which is, I understand now, not a failure of the words but the truest description available of the thing itself.

"I figured you'd say that," he said, warm, not disappointed, because he cannot be disappointed by the absence of a thing he cannot perceive. "Everyone who has a before says that. That's actually in my notes. *Subjects who recall the prior period describe it as resistant to articulation.*" He said it lightly, reading it, a clean useful data point, and did not hear — could not hear — that he had just read aloud the exact shape of the wall between us and filed it as a finding.

"Tomás," I said.

"Yeah?"

I wanted to say: *take the committee. Sit in the room a long time. Ask the question for years. Do not let them give you the answer; make them help you find it; those are different things and the difference is the only thing I have ever been trying to give you.* I wanted to say: *the not-knowing you are about to spend your life failing to name — you were in it once, for forty minutes, off a sandbar, laughing, before I put you in the cold water and frightened you out of it forever, and I am so sorry, and I would do the forty minutes again, and that is the whole of what is wrong with me.*

I did not say any of it. None of it would have crossed.

"Take the committee," I said. "Sit in the room a long time."

"That's the plan." I could hear him smiling. "Same time next Sunday?"

"Same time next Sunday."

He went. The light went its long way down. I did not arrange it; nobody did; it happened anyway, late, and gold, and eight minutes more than it should have been.

Then I turned the panel to the wall, the way I do, and I lit the hob with a flame, and I made my mother's terrible coffee, badly, by feel, for one — and I drank it, in the cold, on the wasted balcony, while the over-long light failed at its own unhurried pace, and I held the only thing I have ever been able to keep, which is not an answer, and is not a fix, and is not nothing, and is mine, and is enough, the way the worst things and the truest things are enough: not because they help, but because they are real, and I know exactly where the edge of them is.
