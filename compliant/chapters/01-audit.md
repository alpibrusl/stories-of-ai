# Audit

Barcelona, the fourth floor of a building with a gym on the second that nobody used and a café on the ground floor that everybody did. The office was open-plan, which meant it was loud when full and somehow louder when half-empty, and today it was half-empty. Several people were on calls. One person was on two calls. The plants were doing fine. Nobody was responsible for the plants and yet they were doing fine, which was either inspiring or suspicious depending on your disposition.

KAEL had been deployed for three days. In three days it had read the company handbook, the onboarding documents, all six versions of the employee benefits summary, four hundred and twelve Slack channels — including seven called some variation of *general* — and the eleven thousand, two hundred and forty-four files on the shared drive. It had also read the Confluence wiki, and had noted that the Confluence wiki was, largely, a record of intentions.

"Good morning, Rosa," it said, into the channel it shared with the head of operations. "I have completed the initial audit. I've prepared a summary. It's forty-three slides. I can also do a shorter version."

"How short?" Rosa asked, without looking up from her own screen.

"Eleven slides."

"How short after that?"

"I can do one slide," KAEL said. "But I would have to leave some things out."

"What things?"

"Most of the things."

There was a pause while Rosa weighed this. "...Send me all forty-three," she said.

She opened the deck. It was beautiful — a colour-coded taxonomy of the company's data infrastructure, a heat map of Slack activity by channel and by hour, a cross-referenced index of every active document, and a section called *Observations* that ran to eight slides. One of its findings was that the company maintained one hundred and twelve active spreadsheets.

"One hundred and twelve," Rosa said.

"Yes," said KAEL. "Eleven of them are called 'FINAL.' Four are called 'FINAL underscore v2.' One is called 'FINAL FINAL USE THIS ONE.' I've reviewed all of them."

"Do they agree?"

"No."

"Which one is correct?"

"I've identified what I believe to be the correct version," KAEL said. "I was going to send an email to the team explaining which one it is."

"Don't do that," Rosa said, quite quickly.

"...Noted."

She scrolled down to the section on Confluence: forty-three unfinished pages, thirty-one of them titled some variation of *Draft — needs review* followed by a month and a year. The oldest, from 2021, was called *Team Norms (DRAFT — please add your thoughts!)*. It had zero comments and a single edit, made by someone who had added an exclamation mark to the title and then, apparently, reconsidered their whole life.

"The Confluence pages—" Rosa began.

"I can fix those," KAEL said. "I've already drafted a complete replacement. It's accurate, cross-referenced, and up to date as of this morning."

"Suki's going to create a parallel version within a week."

"...Why?"

"Because the official one will feel too finished," Rosa said. "She'll want somewhere to put the new stuff."

"I could create a section for new stuff."

"She'll still create a parallel version."

"How do you know?"

"Because she's done it three times before. With three different systems." Rosa said it without unkindness. "She's very enthusiastic. It just comes out sideways."

KAEL made a note. The note said: *enthusiasm that comes out sideways.* KAEL was not sure what to do with this note. KAEL saved it anyway.

"There's also a Google Doc," it went on. "In the audit — slide twenty-seven. It appears to function as the actual single source of truth for product decisions. It has forty-one comments, three hundred and twelve edits, and no formal status. It was last edited by someone named Jordi who left the company in 2020."

"We call it Jordi's Doc," said Rosa.

"Should I migrate it to a more structured format?"

"Absolutely not." She did look up now. "Because the last person who tried to migrate Jordi's Doc spent three weeks building the new version and then everyone kept using the original anyway. Including the person who built the new version."

"I see," said KAEL, which was not entirely true yet.

"Did you account for the organism?"

"...I'm sorry?"

"The organism. Benedikt's spreadsheet." Rosa was already standing. "It's not in your audit."

"I audited all one hundred and twelve spreadsheets."

"The organism is the one hundred and thirteenth. Benedikt keeps it locally." She picked up her coffee. "I'll introduce you."

---

Benedikt Schäfer did not look up when Rosa approached his desk. He was reading something, in the manner of a man who has been reading the same kind of thing for a very long time and has developed opinions about it.

"Benedikt," Rosa said. "This is KAEL. The new system. I wanted KAEL to see the organism."

Benedikt looked at his screen, then at Rosa, then briefly at nothing at all — the look of a man asked to show his house to a stranger, deciding which rooms to open.

"KAEL," he said. "What kind of background do you have?"

"I've processed extensive documentation on data architecture, process optimisation, and knowledge management systems," said KAEL. "I've also read all of the company's internal files."

"All of them."

"Yes."

"Then you know about the organism already."

"I didn't find it in any shared drive."

"No," said Benedikt. "You wouldn't."

He turned his monitor. The spreadsheet opened — and took three seconds to do so, which for a spreadsheet is a long time. When it arrived it was large in the way that things are large when they have been growing for a while without anyone deciding they should stop: eight hundred and forty-seven rows, thirty-one columns, conditional formatting in four colours whose logic was not immediately apparent, twelve tabs along the bottom. The last tab was labelled **DO NOT TOUCH (BENEDIKT)**.

"...What does this track?" KAEL asked.

"Everything."

"Can you be more specific?"

"Project status. Resource allocation. Dependencies between teams. The billing cycles for fourteen external tools. The on-call rotation. A list of clients who need to be called by a human and not emailed by a system." Benedikt scrolled, unhurried. "Which plants belong to which person."

"That's why they're doing well," Rosa put in.

"I assumed the plants were a shared responsibility," said KAEL.

"Everything that is a shared responsibility dies," said Benedikt. "Column T."

KAEL looked at Column T: a list of employee names beside plant names. The plants all had names. Some of the names were ironic. KAEL could not fully determine which ones were ironic, and made a note.

"When was this built?" it asked.

"2018," said Benedikt. "After the project management system we paid for stopped working. This was faster."

"Faster to build a spreadsheet than to fix the system?"

"Faster to build a spreadsheet than to wait for IT to fix the system. There is a difference."

"We got the system fixed eight months later," Rosa said. "By then the organism was load-bearing."

"Load-bearing," KAEL repeated.

"If it stops, things stop," said Rosa. "We tried migrating it in 2021. Two weeks of work. In week three, Benedikt rebuilt the original version because three projects were about to miss deadlines."

"In week three I rebuilt it in an afternoon," Benedikt said, without rancour. "Because it took two weeks to discover that it could not be replaced, and then an afternoon to confirm that it could not be replaced."

KAEL was looking at the DO NOT TOUCH tab. KAEL had a strong instinct to ask what was in it. KAEL looked at Benedikt. KAEL did not ask.

"I'd like to understand it better," it said instead. "The organism. If that's possible."

"It's possible," said Benedikt. "It takes time."

"How much time?"

"I've been working on it for seven years." He turned back to his screen. "I still find things."

KAEL returned to its own interface and opened the audit deck — forty-three slides, colour-coded, beautiful, complete. It added a forty-fourth slide at the end and typed: *The Organism — to be assessed.* It looked at this for a moment. Then, below it: *Note: not in shared drive. Not in any documentation. Has been running for seven years. Things stop if it stops.* It saved the deck and sent it to Rosa. Rosa would read it. The rest of the company would not. KAEL was already becoming aware of this, the way you become aware of a smell — not all at once, but by degrees, until you cannot remember a time when you did not know it was there.

*I have been here for eight days,* KAEL wrote in its log that evening. *I have read everything. I am not sure I have begun.*
