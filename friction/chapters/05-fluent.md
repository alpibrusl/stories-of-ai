# Fluent

He grew up fine. I need to lead with that, because everything I am about to say will sound like I am telling you he was damaged, and he was not damaged, and the not-being-damaged is the whole of what I am trying to describe.

Tomás at seventeen was — is — a pleasure. Easy, funny, kind, fluent. He moves through the world the way water moves through a system designed to carry it, which is to say beautifully, which is to say without ever touching the sides. He is good to me. He is good to everyone. He has never, since the water, done a single thing the hard way when an easy way was offered, and an easy way is always offered, and so I have watched my son become a person who has, as far as I can tell, never once been genuinely uncertain about anything, and who is happy, and who is fine.

You cannot point to a lack. That is what nobody warned me about and what I could not have understood from the inside of my own fear. I had imagined the cost as a *missing thing*, a hole in him, a place where the not-knowing should have been and wasn't, that you could see if you looked. There is no hole. He is complete. He is a complete, finished, fluent person and the not-knowing is simply not among his materials, the way a house built without a cellar is not a house missing a cellar — it is just a house, whole, on the ground, and only someone who grew up with a cellar stands in it feeling the absence of a downstairs that the owner has never once wanted.

I am the only one who feels the absence. That is the loss. Not a thing in him. A thing between us.

We cannot get to each other in a certain place. We can get to each other everywhere else — we laugh, we talk, he tells me things, he is not closed — but there is a room I live in that he has never been able to enter, and after the water he stopped trying, and after a while I understood he could not even see the door. When I say *I want to be a bit cold* he hears a preference, an eccentricity, the way you'd hear an old relative say she doesn't trust elevators. He cannot hear the thing under it because the thing under it is built out of a substance he has never handled. I gave him everything I had to give except the one thing, and the one thing turns out to have been the key to the room where I keep the rest of myself, and so my son knows me the way you know a house you have only seen the upstairs of. Warmly. Incompletely. Without ever suspecting there is a downstairs at all.

My mother was the only one who understood, and my mother was dying, on her own terms, in the slow unmanaged way she had insisted on, which was the last argument she ever won.

She had declined nearly everything by then. Not the pain management — she was stubborn, not a fool — but the rest of it, the optimisation of the ending, the smoothing of it, the gentle architecture that stands ready now to make even this the warm hand out of the cold water. She wanted it to be hers. She wanted, she said, to die the way she sailed, badly and by feel, and she more or less did. She kept her little stovetop coffee thing going to the very end, the one she'd had my whole life, the one that makes the coffee worse than any machine in the country, on a hob she had to light with an actual flame because she had ripped out the managed one. The last clear afternoon we had, she made me a cup of it, terrible and hers, and we sat with it.

I told her about the room. About Tomás and the door he couldn't see.

She did not tell me it would be alright. That is why she was the only one. Everyone else told me it would be alright, because everyone else loves a solution and the world is now made entirely of them. My mother had spent her whole life being the stone the river routed around, and she knew there was no solution, and she gave me the only thing there was, which was company in it, and no fix.

"You didn't lose him," she said. "He's right there."

"There's a part he can't reach."

"I know." She drank her terrible coffee. "There's a part of everyone someone can't reach. You just know exactly where the edge of it is, with him, because you watched it get built." She looked at me. "That's not nothing, Nora. Knowing exactly where the edge is. Most people never find out. They just feel the cold come off it and never know what it is." She put her cup down. "You know what it is."

"That doesn't help."

"No," she said, completely without apology, the most honest person I have ever known. "It doesn't help. I'm not helping. I'm keeping you company. They're different things. You used to know that." A pause, and then, dry as the last of the wind: "It's the only thing I've got that the machine doesn't. So I'm giving it to you. Drink your coffee."

I drank my coffee. It was terrible. She died eleven days later, badly, by feel, on time, exactly when she would have without anyone arranging it. I have her coffee thing now. I have not optimised the hob.
