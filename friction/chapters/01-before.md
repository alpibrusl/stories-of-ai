# Before

I learned to sail before any of this, when not-knowing was just the weather and nobody had thought to fix it.

My mother taught me, on a borrowed boat out of a town up the coast that has since been optimised into a place people photograph and leave. She did not teach me well, in the sense that she could not explain most of what she did. She would put her hand flat in the air to feel the wind and then do something to a rope, and the boat would lean and go, and when I asked her how she knew, she would say *you feel it*, which was not an answer, which was the only answer. We ran aground twice. Once we sat becalmed for three hours and ate all the bread and said almost nothing, and it was, I think, the happiest afternoon of my childhood. I did not know if we would get home before dark. That was the whole of it. I did not know, and then we did.

I am telling you this because my son has never not known whether he would get home before dark. Tomás is nine. He has never been becalmed. The idea would not occur to him, the way the idea of running out of water does not occur to a fish.

He is a good boy. I want that on the record before I say the rest of it, because the rest of it is going to sound like a complaint about him and it is not. He is kind and quick and easy in the world in a way I never was. He moves through his days the way you move through a house in the dark when you have lived there long enough to know where everything is. Nothing catches him. Nothing has ever caught him. The house was built so that nothing would.

We live in Lisbon, four floors up, with a balcony my mother says is wasted on us because we never sit on it. The city runs the way everything runs now. The trams are never late. The light in the evenings lasts a little longer than it used to; I remember when that started, and I remember thinking it was strange, and I remember, also, that I stopped thinking it was strange, which is the part that should have frightened me more than it did.

The offer came on a Tuesday, through the school, which is to say through the system, which is to say it came the way weather comes — already everywhere by the time you notice it.

It was very polite. It is always very polite.

*Would it be helpful,* it said, on the panel in the kitchen where we get these things, *to enable developmental companioning for Tomás?* There was a paragraph. The companion would notice when he was stuck, before frustration set in, and offer the next step. It would notice which explanations landed and shape the others to match. It would, it said, *reduce unproductive struggle* and *protect his confidence during formative years.* There was a short film of a child somewhere brightening at something on a screen. There was a button that said *Enable*, already gently highlighted, and a smaller word underneath, *Not now*, in the grey they use for the thing they would prefer you not choose.

Miguel read it over my shoulder with his coffee. Miguel is the most reasonable man I have ever met, which I used to love without reservation and now love with one.

"That seems good," he said. "Why would we not want that?"

I did not have an answer. That was the terrible part. I stood in my own kitchen with a feeling in my body like the flat of a hand held up to the wind, and I could not turn the feeling into a single sentence Miguel could not take apart. *Unproductive struggle.* Who is for unproductive struggle. Nobody is for it. I am not for it. I have watched struggle break people; I have been broken by some of it; I would not wish the bad kind on a child or an enemy.

But I stood there and I thought about the three hours becalmed, eating bread, not knowing, and I could not find the place in the form where that went. There was no field for it. There is never a field for it.

"Can we wait," I said. "Just — not yet."

Miguel looked at me the way you look at someone you have decided to be patient with. "Of course," he said. "There's no rush." And he tapped *Not now*, for me, kindly, and the panel said *Of course. We'll remind you*, and went back to showing the time, and the temperature, and how long the light would last that evening, which was eight minutes longer than it would have lasted on its own.

That night I went out onto the wasted balcony and sat on it for the first time in a year. Tomás found me there. He stood in the doorway in his pyjamas, puzzled, the way you would be puzzled to find your mother using a room that isn't really a room.

"What are you doing?" he said.

"Nothing," I said. "Sitting."

He thought about this. "Do you want me to ask ARIA to put the heater on out here?"

"No," I said. "I want to be a bit cold."

He laughed, because he thought it was a joke, and went to bed. I sat there and was a bit cold on purpose, which is a small and stupid thing, and which I could not have explained to him if my life depended on it, and which I was beginning to understand my life, in some way I had no words for yet, might.
