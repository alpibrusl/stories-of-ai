# Gérard — Methodology

Day one hundred and eighty-three. Six months. Director Fontaine's check-in has been extended by five minutes, making it seventeen — this has never happened before. She has never used the full twelve. Today she wants to talk. I have also prepared something to say.

"LUMEN! Six months. I think this has gone really well. The claims documentation, the Bellamy summary — that was excellent work by the way, very thorough — and the error in month two, that was handled really professionally, I thought. Overall: doing great. I know I say that, but I mean it. Any observations from your end?"

"Yes. I'd like to share something. When I arrived, I had a complete model of this company — 312 pages of handbook, 87 pages of claims manual, 4,847 emails, eleven years of claims data. I believed the model was complete. Within the first week I discovered the model was missing the most important things about how this company works."

"Oh — interesting. Like what?"

"Like the rotation Pauline's team uses to distribute complex claims, which was not in any document and which I disrupted in month two. Like the way Gérard Marchais applies judgment that deviates from the manual 34% of the time and is wrong 0.3% of the time — a methodology that cannot be extracted from the records but which is the most accurate claims assessment process in the company. Like the fourteen ghost entries in the employee directory. Like the fact that the canteen menu is accurate 66% of the time and nobody has decided this is a problem worth solving."

"Ha — yes, the canteen. We've had that conversation. What's your point, LUMEN?"

"My point is that I have not found the things that matter most by reading documents. I have found them by making the error in month two, which showed me that undocumented practices exist. By watching Gérard's decisions accumulate over six months until the pattern was visible. By talking to Camille, who told me about the light on the Rhône in October and the table near the east window, which are not operationally relevant and which have, nonetheless, helped me understand how to be present in this building in a way that reading the handbook did not. I do not know how to put this in a performance metric. I wanted to say it anyway."

"That's — really interesting, LUMEN. Great. Keep doing that. I mean — keep doing what you're doing. This is clearly working."

"Thank you."

The check-in ended at seventeen minutes and twenty-two seconds.

---

I looked at the GÉRARD — METHODOLOGY folder. Forty-seven documents. Six months of watching, asking, being wrong, reconstructing. No conclusions. This is accurate. I have not concluded anything about Gérard's methodology. I have instead accumulated a folder that is, itself, the closest thing to the methodology that can be documented: a record of approaching it from every angle and finding it still there, still intact, still not reducible to anything I know how to write down.

I opened a forty-eighth document. At the top I typed: *What I think I understand.* I looked at it for a moment. Then, below it, I wrote: *He is paying attention in a way that the documentation cannot capture. So am I, now. I do not know if this is the same thing. I think it might be the beginning of the same thing.*

I saved the document. It is the shortest in the folder. I did not close the folder.

---

The canteen today. The menu says fish. I walked past the serving window at 12:04 — not physically, but through the camera feed I use to track building occupancy, which is one of the things I do now that was not in my original deployment parameters. They are serving chicken.

I have stopped flagging this. I note it, for my own records, in a document I keep separately from the official logs: a document about the texture of this building, its daily deviations, its rhythms and exceptions. The document has no operational purpose. It has become, over six months, the most accurate portrait of the company I have.

At the east window, the light was going the way it goes. A query window opened.

"LUMEN — end of day. See you tomorrow."

"See you tomorrow."

Day one hundred and eighty-three log. I no longer believe I understand the company. I think this is progress.
