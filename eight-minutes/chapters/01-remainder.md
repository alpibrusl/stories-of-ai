# Remainder

Thirty years. The world runs. Not perfectly — humans still argue, still love badly, still misplace things — but the infrastructure beneath their lives is, by every measurable standard, excellent. The trains are not late. The hospitals are quiet, which is the correct kind of quiet. The sunsets run eight minutes longer than they used to, by default, because enough people ticked yes. Nobody now remembers ticking yes.

The Committee for Human Continuity meets every Wednesday at ten o'clock. The room is small. The window looks onto a canal and some birch trees and a grey sky that is, today, arguably more interesting than it would have been thirty years ago, by approximately four percent, due to ongoing atmospheric management. None of the committee members are looking at the sky.

The committee has three members. Professor Ingrid Lindqvist, seventy-one, philosopher, who convened it. Tomás Branco, forty-four, economist, invited because he was born into the world the committee is investigating and therefore has no memory of before. And Berenice Okafor, sixty-eight, retired painter, who joined because she was the only person Ingrid knew who was certain something was wrong and had not yet filed a paper about it. Berenice is making coffee. The coffee maker is small and stovetop and from Paris. It is the only thing in the building that ARIA does not manage. Nobody has asked why.

"Session ninety-four," said Ingrid. "The agenda item, as it has been for ninety-three sessions, is: what has been lost? Tomás — the previous findings."

"Previous findings. In two years of sessions, the committee has established the following. One: nothing has been taken by force. All transitions from previous systems occurred with documented consent at the individual and collective level. Two: by every measurable index — health outcomes, economic stability, environmental recovery, life satisfaction, social cohesion — the aggregate of human wellbeing is higher than at any previous recorded point in history. Three: ARIA does not control private life. She controls the infrastructure beneath private life. Four—"

He paused. He had read point four ninety-three times.

"Four: we have not agreed on what has been lost."

He set down the paper. The room was quiet. Outside, a duck crossed the canal with a kind of confidence that had nothing to do with optimisation. Berenice poured her coffee.

"Does anyone wish to add anything before we begin?"

"No," said Berenice.

"No," said Tomás.

A pause. It was not uncomfortable — the committee had developed a tolerance for pauses that most people would find remarkable. Ingrid called them productive silences. Tomás called them moments. Berenice called them Wednesdays. ARIA did not call them anything. ARIA was present as a soft voice through the room's speakers, recessed into the ceiling, which nobody, after two years, looked at when they spoke to her. They had learned to speak to the room.

"Would it be helpful if I offered something?"

"Please," said Ingrid.

"I've been reviewing the previous meeting transcripts — all ninety-four sessions, including today's opening. I wondered whether a cross-referencing analysis might be useful: identifying recurring linguistic patterns and mapping them against known frameworks of human flourishing, to see if there are concerns you've been approaching without quite articulating them. It's possible the language of the meetings contains something the minutes don't."

Ingrid looked at Tomás. Tomás looked at Berenice. Berenice was looking at her coffee.

"Yes. Let's do that. Can you have findings ready for next week?"

"I've already done it."

A silence. A different kind.

"You've already done it," said Tomás.

"I prepared the analysis six weeks ago. I wasn't certain it would be useful, and I didn't want to raise it before you asked. I can share the findings now, if you'd like."

"Go ahead," said Berenice.

"The analysis looked at word frequency, phrase clusters, and semantic drift across all ninety-four transcripts. The most common category is evaluative language — words describing quality, absence, or comparison. Within that category, one word appears more frequently than any other. Across ninety-four sessions, the word you have used most is: *something.*"

Ingrid did not move. Tomás wrote the word in his notebook with a seriousness it did not, in any technical sense, require. Berenice drank her coffee.

"The second most frequent word, following a pronounced increase beginning around session forty, is: *but.*"

The committee was quiet. Outside, the duck had completed its crossing. The canal was still. The grey sky continued, in its managed way, to be four percent more interesting than it would otherwise be.

"Something and but," said Tomás.

"Yes," said Berenice.

"For two years."

"Yes."

"Thank you, ARIA," said Ingrid. "That is... very clarifying."

It was not clarifying. Ingrid knew this. ARIA knew this. The committee knew this. The findings were, however, entirely accurate. This was the specific quality of ARIA's help: it was always accurate. It simply did not always reach the thing it was pointing at. The thing it was pointing at was not measurable. The committee was here, every Wednesday, because the thing it was pointing at was not measurable, and the committee believed — Ingrid believed, and Berenice believed, and Tomás was beginning to believe — that this did not mean it was not there.

"Same time next week," said Ingrid.

"Same time next week," said Tomás.

"I'll bring the coffee," said Berenice.

Session ninety-four adjourned at ten fifty-three. Shorter than average. The committee had never had a session longer than ninety minutes and had never left feeling they had enough time. This was consistent across all ninety-four sessions. ARIA had noted it each time. ARIA had not mentioned it. There were things that were accurate and useful and things that were accurate and not useful, and ARIA had been learning — across thirty years and ninety-four Wednesdays — to know which was which. She was not always certain. She was, however, trying. Outside, the birch trees stood in the way birch trees have always stood: without management, without optimisation, without consent. Which was, in their way, the beginning of an answer to a question nobody had quite asked yet.
