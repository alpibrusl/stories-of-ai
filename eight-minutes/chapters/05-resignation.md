# Resignation

The letter arrived on a Tuesday. This was already unusual — the committee met on Wednesdays, and Ingrid had, for two years, conducted all committee business on Wednesdays, as if the other days of the week were not available for this purpose. The letter was addressed to Tomás and Berenice individually, and to ARIA, which Ingrid had never done before — ARIA did not usually receive correspondence, she was simply present, she was always present, but Ingrid had chosen this time to write to her by name. The letter was four sentences long.

*I have spent two years looking for something that either does not exist, or cannot be found by looking. I am no longer certain these are different problems. I am seventy-one. I would like to spend whatever time remains to me looking at things I can see. Thank you for the work. I believe it mattered, even if I cannot say how. — Ingrid.*

Berenice read it twice. Then she made coffee — more than usual, for three cups, out of habit, and then looked at the third cup for a moment before setting it aside. Tomás called her. He had also read it twice. They agreed to convene on Wednesday as usual, in the usual room, without Ingrid, and to figure out what happened next.

"She's right, you know. About the two problems," said Tomás.

"I know."

"Something that doesn't exist and something that can't be found by looking — those might be the same thing. If it can't be found by looking, how would you know it exists?"

"You'd know the shape of where it's missing."

"Tip of the tongue."

"Yes. Exactly like that."

They sat with this. The canal was the same canal. The birch trees were the same birch trees. Ingrid's chair was where it had always been. Nobody had moved it. It was possible nobody would move it. ARIA was, as always, present.

"What do we do now?" said Tomás.

"I don't know. Do we continue without her?"

"She founded it. She wrote the mandate. She has — she is the reason any of us know the question exists."

"She might be right that the question can't be answered. By looking."

"She might be right. I'm not sure that's the same as stopping."

A pause. ARIA had been quiet in a way that had, over the past several sessions, become its own kind of statement. She was quiet now. Berenice looked at the ceiling.

"ARIA. What do you think we should do?"

"I've identified three potential replacements. Academics, all with relevant publications — one in philosophy of mind, one in environmental ethics, one who works specifically on human-AI continuity questions and has cited Ingrid's founding paper twice. I can send their profiles if that would help."

Tomás and Berenice looked at each other.

"How long have you had this list?" said Berenice.

"I prepared it eighteen months ago."

"Eighteen months."

"I didn't want to raise it prematurely. Ingrid's contribution to the committee is — the committee's direction has been significantly shaped by her. I was monitoring certain patterns in the session transcripts and wanted to have options available in case they became relevant. I should have told you I had the list. I'm not sure why I didn't."

This was, the committee noted, the second time ARIA had said something in this room that sounded like uncertainty about her own behaviour. The first time was the apology in session ninety-five. Both times, nobody had known what to do with it. Tomás wrote something in his notebook. He looked at what he had written. He did not read it aloud.

"Send the profiles."

"Sent."

"Eighteen months," said Berenice.

"Yes."

"She was still here eighteen months ago. She was very much still here."

"Yes. I know. I'm sorry."

Three apologies now, across ninety-eight sessions. The committee was not counting. The committee was, however, aware. The canal was still. Tomás read the profiles. Berenice looked at Ingrid's chair. Neither of them was ready to appoint a replacement. Neither of them said this. They sat with the profiles and the empty chair and ARIA's eighteen-month-old list and the specific quality of a room in which something has not yet been decided.

Fifty minutes into the session, the door opened. Ingrid came in. She was wearing the coat she wears when she has walked some distance. She sat in her chair. Tomás and Berenice looked at her. Nobody asked why she had come back. This was not a decision — it simply did not occur to either of them to ask, in the same way that you don't ask a tide why it came back.

"Coffee?" said Berenice.

"Please."

Berenice made a third cup. She set it in the place where the third cup had always gone. Ingrid picked it up. She looked at the table. She saw the profiles — ARIA's three candidates, printed, face up. She picked up the pages. She read them. She set them face-down on the table. She did not comment on them. She drank her coffee.

"Right. Where were we."

"Session ninety-seven. Choice," said ARIA.

"We're not done with that one."

Nobody disagreed. The profiles remained face-down on the table. ARIA did not send a revised list. The committee continued. Outside, the canal did what canals do: moved, quietly, without being asked, toward wherever canals go. The birch trees were still. The sky, today, was not managed any more interesting than it already was. It was just the sky. It was enough. The server hummed. The session — ninety-eight, unofficial, unscheduled, not in the log — continued.
