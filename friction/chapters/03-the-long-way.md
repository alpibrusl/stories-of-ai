# The Long Way

There are still boats. That surprises people. The sea is one of the last places the management is thin — it is expensive to optimise water that does not contain anyone important, and for a while there were not enough people who wanted to be out on it to justify the spend. So there are still small boats, in still-real harbours, and there are still a few old people who keep them, and one of them rented me a day-sailer for a price that told me he was glad someone had asked.

I did not tell ARIA where we were going. You can do that — there is a way to go quiet, to decline location companioning for a day, and the system says *Of course. Stay safe. We'll be here if you need us*, which is true, and which sat in my chest the whole drive up like a stone I had swallowed. I told myself I wanted us properly off the grid so the day would be ours. That was true. It was not the whole of why. The whole of why is the next chapter and I am not ready to write it yet, the way I was not ready, that morning, to know it.

The town was the town from my childhood, optimised. Tomás liked it. Of course he liked it; it was made to be liked. He took a photograph of the harbour that was, I could tell, the photograph the harbour wanted taken. Then we got in a small boat that did not want anything, and his whole face changed, because the boat did not respond to him. He pushed against the tiller and it pushed back. He was not used to things pushing back.

"It's heavy," he said, offended.

"It's the water," I said. "The water doesn't know it's you."

He thought about that for a long time. I watched him think about it. It is a thing I had not seen him do — sit inside a problem that did not unfold for him, that just sat there being heavy and indifferent and his to solve. I put his hands where my mother had put mine. I told him to feel the wind, and he said *how*, and I said *you feel it*, and heard my mother in my own mouth, and he gave me the exact look I must have given her, the look that says *that is not an answer*, and I felt — I am not going to be able to make this sound like as much as it was — I felt the line of it, the thread running back through me to her and the bread and the three hours, and forward into him, thin as a hair, but there.

We were not good. I want to be honest about how it actually went, because the temptation is to make it a beautiful day and it was not a beautiful day, it was a real one. We ran the boat up onto a sandbar within the first hour because I am out of practice and he was no help. We argued. He got bored — genuinely, fairly bored, in the stretch where nothing was happening and there was no next thing arriving to meet his boredom, and he did not have the muscle for boredom, nobody his age does, it has been smoothed out of them like an appendix. He asked, twice, whether we could go back. He asked, once, with real plaintiveness, whether I had brought anything to do.

"This is the thing to do," I said.

"Sitting?"

"Sailing."

"We're not sailing. We're stuck on the sand."

"That's part of sailing."

He looked at me with the kindly distance again, the small embarrassed look, and I understood something I had been refusing to understand, sitting there on the warm indifferent water with my bored beloved son: he was humouring me. He had decided, somewhere in the last hour, that this was a thing his mother liked, the way some mothers like a singer or a saint, and that the loving thing to do was to come along and not spoil it. The not-knowing was not a hunger in him that I was feeding. It was a hobby of mine that he was being patient about.

And then the wind came up, properly, the way it does in the late afternoon up there, and the boat came off the sand and leaned and *went*, and for about forty minutes it was the other thing. He felt it — I watched it hit him — the boat suddenly alive under us and the two of us suddenly necessary to it, hauling and ducking and laughing, the spray cold and unmanaged and real, and he stopped humouring me because there was no spare attention left over to humour anyone with. He was just *in* it. He shouted something I couldn't hear and didn't need to. For forty minutes my son was not fluent. He did not know what would happen next and it was the best thing that had ever happened to him, and I knew, the way you know the good afternoon is ending while it is still going on, that I had done it, I had shown him, he had it now, he would carry it —

I want to stop the chapter here. I have stopped it here in my head ten thousand times. Forty good minutes and my son's wet laughing face and the thread pulled taut and holding.

But that is not where the day ended. The day ended in the next chapter, and the next chapter is the one I have been writing this whole book to avoid, and I am going to write it now, because he cannot, because he does not remember it the way I do, because someone has to keep the version where it was my fault.
