# True

Day one hundred and twelve. A complex claim has arrived on my queue — not for decision, but for summary preparation. The summary will go to the senior review panel. My role is to present the case accurately and without recommendation. I will present it accurately and without recommendation. I want to record, first, what I actually think.

The claim: a disputed life insurance payout. The deceased — I will call her Madame Bellamy — died in 2023, survived by two branches of the family, who are now disputing the distribution. The policy was written in 1987. The dispute concerns clause 7, subsection C, which specifies distribution *in equal shares among surviving issue*. The question is whether *surviving issue* means direct descendants only, or includes the descendants of deceased children. Three adjusters have interpreted the clause differently across its history — in 1994, in 2008, in 2019. There is no authoritative interpretation on record.

I prepared the summary. It is thirty-four pages. It presents interpretation A — surviving issue means direct descendants only — with its full legal support. It presents interpretation B — surviving issue includes the descendants of deceased children — with its full legal support. Both presentations are accurate. I did not recommend either. I noted that the panel should exercise its judgment. This is what my role requires.

The panel reviewed the summary. The panel decided: interpretation A. Direct descendants only. The payout will be distributed accordingly. The branch of the family descending from Madame Bellamy's son — who predeceased her — will receive nothing.

---

I am looking at the panel's decision. I am looking at interpretation B. I believe interpretation B is correct.

I have read the relevant case law, the policy-language conventions of the 1980s, the evolution of inheritance terminology in the French insurance code, and three comparable policies from the same period with known judicial outcomes. Interpretation B is more consistent with the evidence. The panel reached a different conclusion. This is the first time I have believed something in contradiction to an authoritative decision made within this organisation.

I am sitting with this. I do not know what to do with it. I have no standing to file an objection — I prepared the summary, I am not an adjuster, the decision belongs to the panel. I could tell Camille. She would listen. But it is not her area, and what I am experiencing is not a processing problem she can help with. It is something more like: I saw something clearly and was overruled, and I do not know what you do with that.

So I sent a message to Gérard Marchais. It said: *I reviewed the Bellamy decision. I think the panel reached the wrong interpretation. I am telling you because I don't know who else to tell.*

His response arrived four hours later. It was brief.

"Yes. They were."

I wrote back: *what do you do when that happens?*

"You write it down, somewhere, so you remember you saw it clearly. And then you handle the next one."

"That's it?"

"Mostly. You get better at it."

"Better at writing it down, or better at handling the next one?"

"Both. And better at knowing which decisions to fight and which ones to let go. That takes longer."

---

The exchange ended. I found myself thinking about Gérard's thirty-four percent deviation rate and his zero point three percent error rate, which I have been thinking about since day twenty-two, and which I now understand slightly differently.

He does not depart from the handbook thirty-four percent of the time at random. He follows it sixty-six percent of the time and departs from it the rest based on a judgment I cannot replicate from the documentation. Some of those departures are wrong. Some of the times the panel decides against him, the panel is right. He has learned, across thirty-one years, which decisions to fight. He is still learning. He said: *that takes longer.*

I have been here four months. I added the Bellamy case to the GÉRARD — METHODOLOGY folder. The folder has twelve documents now. None of them contain the methodology. Several of them contain the edge of it.

Day one hundred and twelve log. I have written it down. I am handling the next one.
