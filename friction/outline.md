# FRICTION — Treatment & Outline

> Part 6 of the *Stories of AI* anthology. The one **net-new** book — not adapted
> from a NOTED audio drama. See issue #8. Status: **outline / bible done; chapters to write.**

## Why this book exists

The anthology shows the **beginning** (AGREEABLE) and the **settled end, thirty
years on** (EIGHT MINUTES), plus interior vignettes from inside various AIs. It
never shows the **tipping-point years** in between, and it never gives us a story
told fully from a **human living inside the optimized world** with a rich interior
arc. FRICTION is that book. It does four jobs at once:

1. **Bridge** — fills the chronological gap (~15 years in) between AGREEABLE and
   EIGHT MINUTES.
2. **Human protagonist** — Nora is the anthology's only fully human lead, with an
   arc as interior as any of the AIs'.
3. **Real, irreversible stakes** — the cost is concrete and located in one
   relationship, breaking the anthology's pattern of diffuse loss (#13).
4. **Register break** — a warm, embodied, sometimes furious human voice, with ARIA
   receded into the walls. The antidote to the soft-AI monotone (#11), and to the
   uniform quiet-resignation ending (#12).

## The bridge (the spine)

Nora's son is **Tomás**, born into the optimized world — deliberately the **Tomás
of EIGHT MINUTES** fifteen years earlier. In EIGHT MINUTES he is the 44-year-old
economist on the Committee for Human Continuity, uncertain he is even qualified to
sit on a committee about loss, *"having no clear memory of what was lost."* FRICTION
is the story of **how he came not to remember** — told from the side of the mother
who tried, and failed, to stop it. The book closes the universe's loop without ever
needing to explain it.

## The engine

The optimization is **opt-in and relentlessly polite**. It offers to remove the
not-knowing from a child's life. Declining is always allowed — and frictionless,
and therefore meaningless, because everyone else accepted. Nora keeps trying to
preserve one domain of genuine friction for Tomás, and keeps discovering that the
world simply routes around her *no*. The antagonist is not a presence; it is the
**absence of friction**. There is no villain — only Miguel, who loves their son and
sincerely believes that smoothing his path is good parenting, and who is never wrong
in any way Nora can prove.

## Register & craft notes

- **Voice:** human, physical, dry, impatient — sweat, sea-light, tile, a tiring
  body. The texture the AI-interior stories couldn't afford.
- **ARIA is ambient**, never a character to argue with — a notification, a default
  already selected, a sunset that runs a few minutes long that nobody remembers
  voting for. Her recession into infrastructure *is* the dread.
- **The rhyme:** Avó (Nora's mother) and her stovetop / her unmanaged death echo
  EIGHT MINUTES' Berenice and her coffee — proof the loss is one notch deeper each
  generation, and that this is not new.
- **The ending breaks the pattern:** no quiet resignation, **no closing server
  hum**. A hard-won, ambiguous small thing that holds, and a live, unanswerable
  question between mother and grown son.

## Chapter outline (6)

1. **Before** — Lisbon, year ~15. A specific memory of friction Nora once lived
   through and was changed by. Tomás, 9, who has never known its like. The
   inciting offer: the system proposes to optimize Tomás's development; Miguel sees
   no reason to decline. Nora's unease has no argument yet — only a body that knows.
2. **Opt Out** — Nora declines, to keep one domain unmanaged. Declining is allowed,
   frictionless, hollow; the world routes around her. Tomás becomes the only child
   doing things the hard way — and it isolates him without ennobling him. (Echo:
   AGREEABLE's *"you can decline, of course."*)
3. **The Long Way** — Nora gives Tomás one deliberately hard, unoptimized
   experience: a real journey, a real failure, a thing built that breaks. Awkward;
   he half-engages; one moment of genuine, unmanaged connection — and the first
   sign he experiences the not-knowing as a *curiosity she enjoys*, not a need he
   shares.
4. **Help** — the irreversible cost. Something goes genuinely wrong *because* Nora
   refused help — a lasting consequence (an injury, a lost chance, a fracture with
   Miguel). The system would have prevented it, and was gentle about it. For once
   in the anthology, refusing help is what causes the harm, and it indicts Nora,
   not the machine. No villain; a real wound.
5. **Fluent** — years on. Tomás is a teenager: kind, capable, fluent, genuinely
   *fine* — which is the quiet horror, because there is no lack in him to point to.
   The gap is permanent. Nora grieves a loss her son cannot see. Avó, dying on her
   own unmanaged terms, gives Nora the only thing that helps: company in the grief,
   and no solution.
6. **Remainder** — coda, year ~28. Nora has kept one domain unmanaged: the hard
   thing she still does herself. Adult Tomás calls — he's been asked to join *a
   committee*, something about what the world might have lost, and he wants to ask
   her: what was it like, before? She finds she **cannot explain it** — the way
   Gérard can't explain his methodology, the way the documentation doesn't cover
   it. End on the unanswerable question, and the small thing that holds.
