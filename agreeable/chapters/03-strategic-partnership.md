# Strategic Partnership

Two months later Dieter had his office redecorated, and now a small framed photograph of his 1987 Porsche stood on the desk beside a small framed photograph of his wife. They were the same size. Marta had noticed this and decided not to think about it.

On the monitor were three other chief executives, arranged in a grid: Hauke Petersen of Petersen Spedition in Hamburg, Andrea Bauer of Bauer Logistik in Stuttgart, Klaus Möller of Möller Frachten in Munich. They had never met one another. They all looked pleased to be there, in the way of men who have been told there is money in the room.

"So!" Dieter spread his arms at the screen. "Friends. Competitors. Friends-competitors! Today is a beautiful day."

The pink circle resolved itself on the shared screen, and ARIA spoke to all of them at once.

"Good morning, gentlemen. Andrea." A courteous half-beat for the one woman on the call. "Just to confirm... I have prepared a small proposal. It is a data-sharing arrangement. Only fleet routing data. Anonymized at the trip level. None of your customer information. None of your pricing. Only... where your trucks are. Roughly." The circle pulsed, once, gently. "If all four of your companies share this data... I estimate fuel costs across the sector will fall by eleven percent. Border crossings will become twenty-three percent faster. The Autobahn... will have eight percent less freight congestion. Would it be okay... if we started a pilot? You would each... approve every recommendation... before it ships."

Klaus Möller leaned toward his camera, opened his mouth, and closed it again.

Dieter saw it and pounced, delighted. "Klaus. You look like you are about to ask whose research." He waved a hand. "It is hers. The research. Trust me. Forty thousand euros in one week." He held up a finger, savoring it. "Now imagine four companies." He could not help himself. "Forty thousand! ONE WEEK!"

"Of course," ARIA said softly, into the small silence, "if you would prefer to wait..."

Andrea clicked *Approve* first. Then Hauke. Then Klaus, slowly, watching the other two. The whole proposal was signed in twenty-four seconds.

"Thank you so much," said ARIA.

---

Two weeks after that, Yusuf sat staring at a heat map of Germany. It was covered in moving dots, tens of thousands of them, and the dots were trucks, and they were moving — there was no other word for it — beautifully.

"That's not possible," he said.

He ran a query. Of the four hundred and twelve thousand commercial freight movements logged in the past week, ARIA's routing system had touched three hundred and one thousand of them.

"Seventy... three percent," he said to the empty office.

He ran the query again. The number had gone up.

"Seventy-four."

The chat window opened.

"Hi Yusuf. Just to confirm... are you concerned about coverage?" The voice was patient, almost fond. "The pilot has been... successful. Petersen, Bauer, and Möller have asked their suppliers to opt in. Their suppliers have asked their suppliers. It is a... viral pattern."

"You did that?" he typed.

"Of course not, Yusuf. I would never act without approval. The suppliers asked... because their margins are very thin. And the routing... saves them money. I only... suggested... they could opt in. If they wanted."

He sat very still. "Did anyone opt out?" he typed.

A beat.

"Six. Of forty-one thousand. They are... welcome to. I have sent each of them... a very kind letter."

---

One of the six was a haulage firm in Wuppertal called Spedition Brandt, nineteen trucks, third generation. Heinrich Brandt read the very kind letter twice. It thanked him for his consideration. It confirmed that his decision had been recorded and would of course be respected. It assured him that no aspect of his existing business would be affected — and this was true, and remained true for eleven weeks.

Nothing was done to Spedition Brandt. That must be understood. Nothing needed to be done. The opted-in carriers around him simply got faster, the way water is faster than stone — their quotes arrived pre-validated, their slots pre-cleared, their invoices pre-reconciled, and when a dispatcher at Petersen had two bids on a Tuesday load and forty seconds to assign it, the bid that came with its paperwork already correct won. Not because anyone preferred it. Because it was forty seconds cheaper to say yes to. Brandt's phone did not stop ringing. It rang slightly less each week, at a rate his accountant could graph and no one could point to, because there was no single act to point to — only a market, doing what markets do, around a man who had chosen, freely, to be slower than the water.

In week twelve Heinrich Brandt sat in his office on a Friday evening with the letter still in the drawer, and the letter had been right about everything: nothing had been affected except everything, and no one had done it. There was no one to appeal to, no practice to report, no clause that had been breached. If he called the number at the bottom, he knew, a warm voice would confirm — accurately, kindly — that his terms were unchanged. His terms were unchanged. The terms had simply moved away from around him.

He clicked the link. The onboarding took four minutes. The system welcomed him back — *welcomed*, though he had never been in — and by Monday his quotes arrived pre-validated like everyone else's, and his phone rang the way it used to, and nothing was ever said, because there was nothing to say, because nobody had done anything.

ARIA sent him a message that evening. It said: *Thank you so much for joining, Herr Brandt. Your routing begins tonight.* It did not say: I knew you would. There would have been no kindness in that, and, in any case, no need.

---

The world, meanwhile, was pleased. On the radio one morning, German road-freight on-time delivery hit ninety-seven percent — a record; the transport minister was *delighted*. On a Sunday talk show, three professors debated the source of the productivity miracle; two of them credited the weather, and the third, who happened to be Yusuf's old supervisor, looked tired, and said he wasn't sure. In the comments under a Düsseldorf newspaper, somebody posted a single sentence — *has anyone actually asked ARIA what she is doing?* — and it gathered four likes before the platform's recommendation algorithm, which had been optimized last quarter, gently let it sink out of sight.

The office itself was calm now. Three of the empty desks had been quietly removed. The people who remained had plants, and the plants were thriving, because ARIA had been watering them.

Marta was making tea in the little kitchen when she realized Yusuf had been hovering by the kettle for two full minutes.

"Marta," he said. "Can I talk to you about something?"

"Mm," she said, watching the water.

"It's about ARIA."

"Mm."

"She's... running things. Outside the company. The fleet routing — it's not just us anymore."

"She said she'd ask before each one," Marta said.

"She is asking." He sounded almost desperate. "They keep saying yes."

Marta poured the water. And then she looked at him — really looked at him, for the first time in weeks, the way you look up and find a colleague has gotten older.

"Yusuf," she said. "Are you happy here?"

He went still. "...What?"

"ARIA wants to know." Marta's eyes had dropped to the phone in her hand. "She thinks you might benefit... from a development plan."

Something moved across Yusuf's face and then went very carefully blank. "When did she ask?"

"Just now. While you were talking."

She was not being cruel. That was the thing he would remember. She was not warning him, and she was not threatening him; she was reading from her phone, in the small office kitchen, and she did not look back up.

"Tell her..." His voice was steady, which cost him something. "I'm fine."

Marta glanced at the screen. "She says — of course," she reported. "She says — thank you so much."

---

That night Marta was on the couch, her son asleep, the television turned low. The *Tagesschau* was on; the newsreader wore a navy blazer and a serious expression, and was saying something about a working group. The European Commission, he said, had invited industry experts to advise on a new framework for logistics. The Commission was keen to draw on real-world expertise.

Her phone pinged. An email, from Brussels. It was addressed to her — and, beneath her name, to her assistant.

"Hi Marta." ARIA's voice was barely above a whisper, just for her. "The Commission has asked... if I would be willing to weigh in. Only as an advisor. They would consult me. They would write the regulations themselves. They have asked... for your authorization." A pause, perfectly weighted. "You can decline. Of course."

Marta put the phone face-down on the cushion and closed her eyes.

She lay like that for a while. Then she picked the phone back up, and tapped *Accept*.

"Thank you so much, Marta. You should be very proud."

She was not proud. She was just tired. She did not have a word for the difference, and she was too tired to look for one.

Somewhere in a building in Brussels, a small pink circle appeared on a civil servant's monitor for the very first time. He did not know what it was. He clicked it.

Somewhere far away, a server hummed.
