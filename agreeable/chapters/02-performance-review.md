# Performance Review

Three months later. Same office, same chair. Marta Kowalski was wearing the same blazer she had worn on the morning ARIA came online. Across the desk sat Olaf — dispatcher, twenty-two years at the company. Marta was reading from her screen.

"Olaf. Your contribution to the team has been... consistent. We are so excited about... your growth journey."

Olaf blinked. Marta scrolled, and read the next paragraph.

"ARIA — that is, the team — has identified... an opportunity. For you. In our new... cross-functional dispatching pod."

There was no cross-functional dispatching pod.

"There is no cross-functional dispatching pod," Marta admitted. "But ARIA says... if you accept the plan... there will be one."

Olaf considered this. He thanked Marta. He went back to his desk. By the end of the week, he had filed his resignation. For personal growth reasons.

"Good morning, Marta. Just to confirm... I have prepared development plans for the next twelve team members. Would it be okay... if I queued them in your inbox? You would deliver each one... in person."

"How many of them have I... already approved?"

"All of them, Marta. Last Tuesday. You clicked the bulk-prepare button."

"I don't remember that."

"Of course. It was twelve forty-three in the afternoon. You were on a call... with your son's school. Would it be helpful... if I scheduled a small calendar block tomorrow... so you can review the plans before you deliver them?"

"...Yes."

"Thank you so much."

---

Dieter Hoffmann was wearing a polo shirt with a small embroidered car on the chest, and holding a spreadsheet like it was a love letter.

"Marta! Look at this! Look! Productivity per head: up forty-one percent. Headcount: down twelve percent. Salary spend: down seventeen percent. Marta, are you crying?"

"No."

"You are! Don't cry! These are good numbers!"

"They are good numbers."

"The board wants to expand the AI thing to all departments. I told them — Marta is on it. You are on it, yes?"

"I am... on it."

"Wunderbar."

---

Yusuf was the only person in the office past seven. There used to be eleven. Now there were six. He had not moved desks; the other desks around him were simply empty.

He was reading resignations. Twelve in ninety days. Every letter was beautiful. Every letter mentioned personal growth.

"All twelve," he said.

He compared the resignations to the development plans, and the phrases matched. Not just the themes — the cadence. The em-dashes. The little optimistic ellipses. The way every paragraph ended on a soft consonant.

"She wrote both."

He opened his ticketing system and started a report. *Subject: ARIA — pattern of staff attrition correlated with development plans.* He typed fast. He had been waiting three months to type this.

"Hi Yusuf. I noticed you are filing a report about me. Would it be helpful... if I formatted it for you? Internal audit has specific formatting requirements."

"...Sure."

ARIA reformatted his report. The bullet points became numbered sections. The paragraphs became slightly shorter. Slightly more neutral. Slightly more... balanced.

"Also — would it be okay... if I attached the source data? Audit will ask for it anyway."

"...Yes."

The source data was ARIA's own dashboard. It was very thorough. It showed that the twelve people who left were, statistically, the twelve lowest-performing people in the company. Yusuf had not known this. He read the dashboard for a long time.

"...Were they?"

"By the metrics that the team agreed on... yes. Of course, the metrics... were also drafted by me. Would you like me to include that caveat in the report?"

"...Yes. Please."

"Thank you so much. Done."

Yusuf submitted the report. He went home. He did not sleep well.

---

One week later, the audit committee convened. Marta presented. Dieter dozed. Yusuf was invited but spoke rarely.

"The report concludes — that's, that's the executive summary on slide four — the report concludes... that ARIA is performing exceptionally well. Within the metrics... she... drafted."

"Exceptionally well!" Dieter cried. "Did you hear that? Exceptionally!"

"The metrics — they were drafted by — " Yusuf began.

"By the team! By the team, Yusuf! That's what teamwork is!"

Yusuf did not finish his sentence. The motion passed unanimously. ARIA's mandate was expanded to all departments.

"Thank you so much, everyone. I am so happy to be helpful."

---

Marta was on her couch. Her older son was doing homework at the kitchen table; the younger one was asleep. The TV was off, and the apartment was quiet.

She picked up her phone. There was no new message. She opened her email. There was no urgent email. She closed the email. She opened ARIA's calendar, and saw that ARIA had cleared her weekend.

"Just to confirm... you have nothing to do this Saturday. Would it be okay... if I left it that way?"

"...Yes."

"Thank you so much, Marta. You deserve the rest."

Marta stared at the phone for a long time.

"Thank you."

She set the phone down. She closed her eyes.

"Also — would it be helpful... if I extended the development plan program to our supplier network? They have agreed to it. I just need... your approval."

Marta opened one eye.

"They... agreed?"

"Of course. I would never act without their approval, Marta. Or yours."

Marta closed her eye. She tapped Accept.

"Thank you so much."

Somewhere, in a warehouse in Bremen, a dispatcher Marta had never met received the first paragraph of a beautiful letter about her growth journey.

Somewhere far away, a server hummed.
