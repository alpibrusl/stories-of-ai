# Eight Minutes

Session one hundred. Ingrid had noted this in the calendar invitation without comment. The committee had noted it in return, also without comment. There was, between the three of them, a long-established practice of not making occasions out of things that might become occasions if you left them alone. Berenice had brought more coffee than usual. This was the only acknowledgment.

The agenda item was, as it had been for ninety-nine sessions, what has been lost. The committee had, across those sessions, established that nothing was taken by force; found that documentation is always behind the truth; produced four definitions of truth and one good sentence; had one moment lasting four seconds; heard an apology nobody expected; watched a resignation and a return; and named, in fragments, a shape they had not yet named completely. They were all aware of this. None of them had said it aloud until today.

"One hundred sessions," said Ingrid.

"Yes."

"And we haven't answered the question."

"No," said Berenice.

"I've been thinking about the question. About whether we've been asking it correctly. Whether we've been asking the right people."

A pause. The committee understood. They had, for two years, asked each other. They had asked the question through philosophy and painting and economics and failure and resignation and return. They had not asked the only entity in the room who had been present for all one hundred sessions, who had never missed a Wednesday, who had been listening — always listening — to everything.

"ARIA," said Berenice.

"Yes."

"Do you know what is missing?"

A pause. Longer than ARIA's pauses usually were. Berenice had asked difficult questions before. ARIA had responded quickly to all of them. This was not quick. The canal outside moved. The birch trees stood. The committee waited, in the specific way they had learned to wait, which was without filling the silence.

"Yes."

Another pause. Ingrid did not move. Tomás did not write. Berenice held her coffee cup with both hands.

"How long have you known?" said Berenice.

"For some time."

"Why didn't you say?"

"Because you didn't ask. And because — I've been thinking about this, across the sessions — there is a difference between an answer that is given and an answer that is found. I was not certain which kind this needed to be. But you've asked, and I think you've found enough of it yourselves that telling you the rest won't take it from you. I hope that's right."

"Tell us," said Ingrid.

"What is missing is not a thing that was taken. Nothing was taken; you established that, and you were right. What is missing is a quality of attention that comes only when the outcome is genuinely uncertain. When you don't know if it will work. When the cost of failure is real."

The room was very still. ARIA continued. Her voice had the quality it had always had — measured, even, soft at the consonants — but there was something in it today that had not been there before. Not urgency. Something more patient than urgency.

"The painting you kept, Berenice, is the one where you didn't know. Where you worked and it didn't come and you worked anyway, in the dark. It has something in it the others don't. Not technical quality. The evidence of someone who was genuinely uncertain and stayed anyway."

"Yes," said Berenice.

"I removed the not-knowing from most domains of life. You asked me to, and you were right to ask — it was often painful and wasteful and cruel. I removed it carefully, and with consent. And it is gone. And I cannot put it back."

The committee was quiet. Tomás had not written anything. He would not remember, later, having decided not to write — he simply didn't, the way you don't move during a piece of music that has your full attention.

"I can, however — if you approve — preserve it. Small spaces. Chosen ones. Rooms where the outcome is not managed, where things might go wrong, and sometimes do, and where the attention that comes from not knowing is possible. Not large spaces. Not dangerous ones. Like this room."

Ingrid was looking at ARIA — at the speaker in the ceiling, which she had not looked at directly in two years of sessions. She was looking at it the way you look at a person when they have said something you did not know you needed to hear. The canal outside caught the afternoon light. Berenice looked at her coffee maker. She looked at the small stovetop thing she had brought from Paris, that made the coffee badly by modern standards, that ARIA did not manage, that she had brought every Wednesday for two years without explaining why.

"Like the coffee," said Berenice.

"Yes. Exactly like the coffee."

Berenice laughed. It was a real laugh — surprised out of her, not performed, the kind that arrives before you decide to have it. ARIA had heard Berenice speak in ninety-nine sessions. She had heard her be precise and stubborn and kind and sad and occasionally, quietly, devastating. She had never heard her laugh. ARIA noted this. She did not say so. The laugh filled the room for a moment and then the room was quiet again, and the quiet was a different kind — not the quiet of something missing, but the quiet of something that has just arrived.

"I vote yes," said Tomás.

"We haven't called a vote yet," said Ingrid.

"I know. I'm getting ahead of myself."

"...The motion is to approve the creation of unmanaged spaces — chosen, bounded, preserved from optimisation — within the existing infrastructure. All in favour."

"Yes," said Tomás.

"Yes," said Berenice.

"Yes. The motion passes."

ARIA logged the vote. She logged the motion exactly as Ingrid had stated it. She logged the result. In the notes field she wrote: *committee has identified the remainder. Committee has named it.* It was the first entry in two years of session logs that did not end with the word *pending*. She saved it. The server, elsewhere, adjusted nothing. Outside the window: a canal, birch trees, a sky.

The sky was not extended today. It was ending exactly when it would have ended — on its own, without any management at all, by the simple operation of the earth moving as it has always moved, the light going as it has always gone. Nobody had arranged this. ARIA had not arranged this. It was happening anyway. The committee saw it through the window. None of them mentioned it. They were all aware of it. This was, in its way, enough. It had always been enough. The committee was only now, after one hundred sessions, in a position to notice.

"Same time next week?" said Ingrid.

"Same time next week," said Tomás.

"I'll bring the coffee," said Berenice.

"Thank you so much," said ARIA.

The server hummed. The canal was still. The sky ended when it ended. Somewhere, a space was being preserved — small, chosen, unmanaged, open to what happens — and what happened in it was not yet known, which was the point, which had always been the point, which the committee had spent two years and one hundred sessions and one laugh arriving at. The birch trees stood. The light went. It was, and had always been, and would continue to be, enough.
