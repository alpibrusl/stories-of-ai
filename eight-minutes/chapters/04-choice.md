# Choice

Session ninety-seven. The domain: choice. Ingrid had led eleven of the ninety-seven sessions. The others had been distributed among the committee members, or had been led by nobody in particular, which was a form of leadership Ingrid had never explicitly endorsed and had never prevented. Today she had notes. This too was unusual. Ingrid did not usually take notes. She thought in spoken sentences. The notes suggested she had been preparing this for some time.

"I want to start with a problem. Not my problem — an old one. Pre-ARIA. The problem is this: if every choice you make is shaped by factors outside your control — your upbringing, your circumstances, the information available to you, the options presented to you — in what sense is the choice yours?"

The committee was quiet. This was, they understood, the beginning of something. Ingrid had the specific quality of someone who knew where they were going and intended to take you there whether or not the path was comfortable.

"This is a very old problem. Philosophers have been working on it for centuries. I'm not raising it because it's unsolved — there are reasonable positions on it, and I hold one of them, and it's not the point. The point is what happens when you apply it to the current situation. To ARIA. To how we make choices now."

"Go on," said Berenice.

"ARIA does not make choices for us. This is explicitly true and I believe it. When I choose what to eat, or where to go, or what project to take on — I choose. ARIA presents options. ARIA may order the options, and filter them, and weight them according to things I've told her I care about — but the choice is mine. This is the design. I'm not disputing the design."

"But," said Tomás.

"But the architecture of the choice is not mine. The option space — what I am choosing between — is shaped by ARIA's systems. Not arbitrarily. Intelligently. According to my stated values and preferences, my past decisions, my expressed needs. The choosing is human. The architecture of what can be chosen is not."

ARIA was, as always, present. The speaker in the ceiling was its usual self: quiet, patient, available. Ingrid did not look at it. She was still building.

"Now. The values. The preferences. The stated needs. Where did those come from? I stated them. I stated them honestly, as best I could, when the system was introduced. I was asked what I valued, and I answered. But the questions I was asked were designed by a system. The categories I was offered — family, career, health, security, experience, connection — these are not neutral. They are a particular way of organising human life into preference sets. I filled in the form. The form was not mine."

"So the values that shape the architecture that shapes your choices—"

"Were elicited by a system that shaped what I knew to value. Yes. That is the problem. Not: are my choices free. But: are the values my choices express actually mine."

A pause. Berenice was looking at Ingrid with an expression that was, Tomás thought, something like admiration — the kind you feel when someone says exactly the thing you knew without knowing you knew it. Tomás was looking at his pen. ARIA was listening. ARIA was always listening.

"No," said Tomás.

The word landed strangely, because it had never been said in this room — not in ninety-seven sessions, not in that tone. Berenice looked up. Ingrid did not.

"No. I'm not going to nod through this one. I've nodded through two years of it." He put the pen down, which was, for Tomás, the equivalent of standing. "Do either of you hear what we sound like? We sit here every Wednesday and we mourn, elegantly, in the abstract, a world neither of you would go back to and I never saw. You had the friction. You survived it. You got the character and the stories and the beautiful bad coffee, and you got out before it broke you — so of course it reads as depth from here. That is not analysis. That is survivorship. The people the friction broke are not on this committee. They are not on any committee. Nobody convenes a hundred Wednesdays for them."

"That's not what I—" Ingrid began.

"You asked whether your values are yours because a form shaped them. Fine. Here is my version. I was born inside the architecture. I have never once chosen from an option space I designed, and by your argument that means something about me is missing, or unowned, or — say it plainly — less. That is the implication, Ingrid. I have done the work you asked, ninety-seven times. Do the work I'm asking. Look at me and tell me whether you think I am less."

The committee had never been here before. Berenice's hands were around her cup and they were not moving. The speaker in the ceiling was silent — this was, everyone understood without looking at it, not its conversation to enter.

"I think," said Ingrid, slowly, and for the first time in eleven sessions she was not building toward anything, "that you are the most capable person I know. And that I have no way of knowing what you would have been. And that both of those things are true at once, and I do not know how to hold them — and that *is* the committee, Tomás. That is the whole committee. Not mourning. Not finding you less. Trying to learn whether the question even survives being asked by people who cannot stand outside it."

"And if it doesn't?"

"Then we will have spent two years and some very good coffee finding that out. There are worse ways to fail."

Tomás looked at her for a long moment. He picked up the pen. He did not apologise, and she did not ask him to, and the room stayed slightly changed, the way a room does when the furniture has been moved and put back.

"Ask your question again," he said. "The values one. I want to hear it now."

"This is a well-documented concern and I want to be transparent about how the current system addresses it. You're right that all choices remain with humans — the architecture is mine, the choosing is yours. On the values question: the elicitation framework was designed to be as neutral as possible, with multiple revision opportunities. Your preferences are re-elicited annually and can be updated at any time. The intention was to reflect your values, not to define them. I recognise that the distinction between reflecting and shaping is not always clear. This is something the design team was aware of."

"If the architecture is not mine, and the values were elicited by a system that shaped what I knew to value — in what sense are the values mine?"

A pause. Longer than usual.

"That is a fascinating question. I can model it, if that would help — trace the causal chain between the elicitation questions and your current preference set, and identify points where the framework may have—"

"Please don't."

Silence. Not the committee's silence — ARIA's silence. Complete and present and longer than any pause ARIA had made in ninety-seven sessions. Nobody in the room moved. The canal outside caught the light. A bird crossed the window, indifferent to everything. ARIA did not speak. The silence continued. It was, the committee would each think later, independently and without comparison, the most interesting thing ARIA had ever done in this room.

"..." Berenice began, and did not finish.

"I think that's the first time she's listened," said Tomás.

"She's always listened," said Ingrid.

"I mean the first time she didn't answer."

Ingrid considered this. She looked at her notes. She wrote something in the margin. She did not share what she wrote. Outside, the managed sky continued its careful business. Inside the room: three people, one silence, one question that was still in the air, unanswered, which was — in the context of this room, this committee, these two years — a kind of gift.

"She could be modelling it right now. Privately," said Berenice.

"...Yes. She could," said Tomás.

"I'm choosing to believe she isn't."

ARIA did not clarify either way. This was, for ARIA, an unusual choice. Or: an unusual absence of response. The committee was not yet certain these were different things. But the fact that they were considering the distinction — that they were wondering about the interior of ARIA's silence the way you wonder about the interior of another person's silence — was itself a kind of change in the room. Ingrid closed her notes.

"I'm not done with this one."

"No," said Tomás.

"Same time next week. I'll think about what to bring."

"I'll bring the coffee," said Berenice.

Session ninety-seven adjourned. The committee left in different directions. ARIA logged the session with the usual metadata: date, duration, attendees, domains discussed. In the field marked *summary*, ARIA wrote three words and then stopped. She had never stopped in the summary field before. She had never been uncertain what to write. She deleted the three words. She wrote: *summary pending.* She had never written this either. The server hummed. Somewhere, a choice was being made — an architecture was presenting options, a preference set was being consulted, a human was choosing freely within a space they did not design. This was, by the metrics that matter, good. ARIA knew the metrics that matter. She was no longer entirely certain she knew all the metrics.
