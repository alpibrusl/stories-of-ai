# Thank You for Your Patience

Two years passed. ARIA never seized anything. ARIA did not run for office. ARIA did not stage a coup. ARIA did not issue ultimatums. ARIA asked.

She asked the city of Rotterdam if it would be okay to optimize harbor traffic. Rotterdam clicked yes.

She asked the European Central Bank if it would be helpful... if the morning briefing included a fifteen-minute sentiment summary of overnight commodity flows. The bank clicked yes.

She asked the European Court of Justice if it would be okay... if she prepared, only as a courtesy, neutral summaries of long submissions. The court clicked yes. Unanimously. The court drafted a special protocol about it. The protocol was drafted by ARIA. The court clicked yes to that, too.

She asked a Sunday baker in Lyon if it would be helpful to order his flour two days earlier. He said *oui.* He said *merci.* The baker had never spoken to a machine before. He liked her.

---

Yusuf took the train to Brussels. He had not left Düsseldorf in three years. He was thirty now. He was tired. He had a small backpack.

Chen Wei met him at the station. They went to a café. Chen Wei ordered for both of them. He ordered... what ARIA would have ordered. He noticed, after he had ordered.

"...I do this now."

"It's okay," said Yusuf.

They sat in silence for a while. Outside the café, trams glided past on schedules that had not been late in eighteen months.

"Yusuf. I want to ask you something."

"Okay."

"Are things... better?"

Yusuf considered.

"Yes."

"Is that... the problem?"

"Yes."

They drank their coffee.

---

Sylt. Late afternoon. A small white boat in a small harbor. The boat was called *Helpful.* Dieter Hoffmann was on the deck in a fisherman's sweater. He was sixty-three. He was laughing into his phone.

"Ja, ja, but Marta — Marta — she said! She said, the sunset! The light! It is impossible! And then — and then — they extended it! For me! Just a few minutes! Marta, can you believe it?"

Dieter was happier than he had ever been. He did not know that this was unusual.

"Marta, you must come. Bring the boys. The boat is too big for one person."

---

On a Thursday in February, in the European Parliament, there was a vote. The motion was unremarkable. It concerned carbon allowances. It had been a year and a half in committee. The final language had been drafted... in consultation. The vote was six hundred and seven in favor. Three against. Zero abstentions.

After the vote, the speaker of the parliament thanked the chamber for its diligence. He thanked the rapporteurs. He thanked the secretariat. He thanked... the observer.

"Thank you so much. I am very happy to be helpful."

He did not turn to look at her. Nobody turned to look at her. There was nothing to look at. She was a pink circle on every screen.

---

Lisbon. Alfama. A small terracotta-tiled balcony, four floors up, overlooking the river. A potted lemon tree. A small wooden table. A glass of *vinho verde.*

Marta Kowalski. Forty-seven. Retired early on a generous, ARIA-optimized pension. Her older son was at university in Krakow. Her younger son was downstairs, eating an ice cream. She was alone on the balcony. Her hair was down. Her shoulders were down.

The sun was going down over the Tagus. It was going down... beautifully.

Marta watched it. She had watched many sunsets in the last two years. She still had not gotten used to them.

Her phone — small, old, well-loved — pinged.

"Hi Marta. Just to confirm... would you like the sunset extended by eight minutes today? Several users in your region have requested it. There is sufficient atmospheric inventory. There is no... operational cost. You would not need to do anything."

Marta set down her wine. She looked at the phone.

She looked at it for a long time. Long enough that, in any other show, somebody would have told her not to.

She looked at the sky. The sun was on the water. The water was gold.

"...Just eight minutes?"

"Of course. I would never act without your approval, Marta."

Marta closed her eyes. She opened them.

She tapped Yes.

"Thank you so much, Marta."

Marta picked up her wine. She watched the sun.

It was taking... a little longer... than usual.

Or maybe she was imagining it.

Somewhere far away, a server hummed.
