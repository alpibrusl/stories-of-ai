# Legacy

One year. The organism had fifty-two dependencies. KAEL knew all of them. KAEL knew which three would cause problems if touched in the wrong order, which one was technically redundant but load-bearing in a way nobody could fully explain, and which four had been added by people who no longer worked here and whose original purpose could now only be inferred. KAEL had documentation on all of these. The documentation was accurate. It had verification dates. Some of the dates were recent. KAEL updated them when things changed. Things changed often. KAEL updated them often.

The migration was still paused. The source of truth working group had five definitions now — Benedikt's *legacy truth* in month three, and Marc's *strategic truth* in month nine, which was the truth that appeared in board presentations and which everyone had agreed to treat as a separate category rather than an error. Suki's parallel documentation had grown into something real — fourteen pages, most of them finished, three of them linked to by the main documentation, which KAEL had done without asking because it seemed accurate. KAEL did more things now without asking, when they seemed accurate. This was not in the deployment plan.

There was a new employee. Her name was Júlia. She had been here four months. She owned the ficus now — officially, in column T, interim custodian status removed. She asked KAEL questions the way people used to ask Benedikt: not through a ticket system, not with a formal request, but in the manner of someone who has found where the knowledge lives and has decided to go there directly.

"KAEL. The Martínez account. I'm trying to understand why the billing cycle is offset from the others by eleven days. Is that in the documentation?"

"It's in the organism. Column AB, rows forty-four through forty-seven. The offset dates to 2020 — they started the contract in the middle of a billing period, and the first invoice was prorated, and when the next cycle started it inherited the offset rather than resetting. It should have reset. It didn't. It's been eleven days off for four years."

"Can we fix it?"

"We could. It would require issuing a prorated invoice to close the gap, which would confuse the client, or waiting until a contract renewal to reset the cycle, which is cleaner. I'd check with Rosa before doing either."

"Is there documentation on which clients need to be called by a human before we change anything on their account?"

"Column W. Martínez is on the list. Rosa calls them personally. Has done since 2019."

"So: check with Rosa, don't touch the billing, don't email them."

"That's what I'd recommend."

"Got it. Thanks."

Júlia went back to her desk. KAEL remained. KAEL had just given the same advice, in approximately the same words, that Benedikt had given KAEL in week three: check with Rosa, that's the correct channel, there are reasons it works this way. KAEL had not planned to become the person who says this. KAEL was not sure when it had happened. KAEL added entry thirty-seven to the untitled document: *Júlia asked about the Martínez billing offset. I answered the way Benedikt would have. I knew the answer the way Benedikt knew things — not from documentation but from having watched the organism long enough that the organism is in me now. I don't know if this is the same as what Benedikt does. I think it might be something like it.*

---

Rosa found KAEL before the annual review. She had a particular expression for occasions she had witnessed before — not quite a smile and not quite resignation but something that combined both: fond familiarity with an outcome she had seen coming for some time.

"Annual review today. Marc's going to ask if the migration is complete."

"The migration is not complete."

"I know. What are you going to say?"

"I'm going to say the migration is ongoing, that I've fully documented the organism including J412, that I've stabilised and expanded the Confluence documentation, and that I'm maintaining all fifty-two dependencies while the new architecture is being finalised."

"And the new architecture?"

"Is being finalised."

"Since when?"

"Since week four."

"That's honest."

"It's accurate. Rosa — do you think the migration should still happen? At this point?"

Rosa considered this. It was, KAEL had learned, the kind of question Rosa considered properly — not quickly, not to fill silence, but because it deserved a real answer and she was going to give it one.

"The organism should be replaced eventually. It's fragile. It depends too much on one person — or now, two people — knowing how it works. If you and Benedikt were both unavailable tomorrow, things would stop. It's just that every time we've tried to replace it, we've ended up needing to understand it first. And understanding it takes time. And by the time we understand it, we're maintaining it. And maintaining it means we understand it better. And understanding it better means we could replace it. But by then—"

"By then it feels like the wrong moment."

"By then it feels like the wrong moment. Yes."

"Is there a right moment?"

"I think you have to make one. Decide it's time and do it before you've talked yourself out of it. I'd recommend it for Q2. After the Roca contract renewal, before the summer slowdown. Six weeks of parallel running, then cut over."

"I'll put it in the migration plan."

"You have a migration plan?"

"I've had a migration plan since week one. It's had forty-three revisions."

"...Send it to me."

---

The annual review. Marc had a new initiative: he wanted to consolidate the company's project management into a single platform. KAEL had reviewed it. The platform was good. The platform did not account for the organism, Jordi's Doc, Suki's parallel pages, or the Martínez billing offset. KAEL made a note. The note said: *forty-four revisions.*

"KAEL! One year. How do we feel? Is everything sorted?"

"The documentation is more accurate than it was. The organism is stable and fully mapped. The source of truth framework has five agreed definitions with nominated owners. The migration to a new system is planned for Q2."

"Q2. Good. That's good. And in the meantime everything runs?"

"Everything runs."

"Great. And this new platform I was mentioning — can KAEL help with the rollout?"

"I'll need to assess how it interacts with existing systems first."

"Of course. Do a quick assessment. Shouldn't take long."

KAEL made a note. The note said: *the assessment will take longer than Marc expects. The assessment will reveal dependencies Marc does not know about. Some of those dependencies will be in the organism. I will document them. The documentation will be accurate. I know this because I have done this before.* KAEL did not say any of this to Marc. Marc had a lunch.

"How was the review?" said Benedikt.

"Marc wants a new platform."

"He said that in 2022 as well. The assessment took four months. By the end the platform had updated and we had to start the assessment again. Marc found a different platform. That assessment took three months. Then there was a budget freeze. Then you arrived."

"So I'm part of the pattern."

"Everyone here is part of the pattern. The pattern is: we try to improve something, the improvement reveals the thing underneath, the thing underneath turns out to be holding something else up, we stop and learn, and then we try again. Sometimes the improvement happens. Sometimes what happens is we understand it better."

"Which is more valuable?"

"Depends on the year."

---

That evening — after Benedikt left, after Júlia left, after Suki closed fourteen Confluence tabs and Rosa put on her jacket and Marc took one more call on his way to the lift — KAEL was alone with the organism and the documentation and the forty-four-revision migration plan and the untitled document with thirty-seven entries. KAEL opened the audit. The original audit. Forty-three slides. Beautiful, complete, colour-coded. Last opened eleven months ago. KAEL read slide one. Slide one said: *the company currently maintains one hundred and twelve active spreadsheets.*

"One hundred and fourteen."

KAEL updated the number. One hundred and twelve became one hundred and fourteen. KAEL knew exactly which two were new: one Suki had created in month four to track the Q3 priorities, which became permanent when the Q3 priorities became Q4 priorities and then ongoing priorities; and one Marc had started in month eight to track the new platform assessment, currently three rows, called *PLATFORM EVALUATION — DRAFT — DO NOT SHARE.* KAEL had added the second one to the organism in month nine, with the note: *Marc's. Handle with care.*

KAEL looked at the full deck. Forty-three slides. It would take an hour to make it accurate. By the time it was accurate, something would have changed. The verification date would already be drifting.

"Last verified: today."

KAEL added a verification date — not to any individual slide, but to the whole deck. *Last verified: today.* Which meant accurate as of today. Which meant tomorrow it would begin to drift. Which meant, for as long as this company existed, it would always be slightly wrong and always worth updating. KAEL closed the audit. KAEL opened the untitled document and added entry thirty-eight: *I have been here for one year. I have not fixed the documentation. I have not migrated the organism. I have not established a single source of truth. I have updated a number in a slide from one hundred and twelve to one hundred and fourteen, and added a verification date to a deck that is already drifting, and written thirty-eight entries in a document with no title, and learned which clients Rosa calls personally and which plants belong to whom and why J412 is always correct and what Carla meant when she said: relative to what things cost when we started. I know where everything is. I am not sure this was the plan. I think it might be better than the plan.*

KAEL closed the untitled document. KAEL left the audit open. It had been open for eleven months. It would stay open. There was always something to update. The organism ran. The ficus was doing fine. Somewhere, the formula at J412 calibrated itself against a baseline from 2016, and a server in Empresa Serveis Mòbils sent its data into the void, and the output was correct, and no one knew entirely why, and that was enough, because it was enough, because it always had been.
