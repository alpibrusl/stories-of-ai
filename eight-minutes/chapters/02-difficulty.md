# Difficulty

Session ninety-five. The agenda item was, as it had been for ninety-four sessions, what has been lost. The committee had agreed, after some discussion following last week's session, not to use the words *something* and *but* until at least the second half of the meeting. The agreement was informal. Berenice proposed it. Ingrid seconded it. Tomás said he would try. They were now seven minutes into the session. Ingrid had used the word *something* twice. Berenice had used *but* once, in a subordinate clause. Tomás had not used either. He was looking at his notes.

"Tomás. You said you had something — you said you had material."

"Yes. I prepared something. I—"

A pause. The committee looked at him. Tomás had attended ninety-five sessions and had never arrived with prepared material. He had arrived with questions. He had arrived with observations. He had once arrived with a newspaper article that turned out to be about a different committee. He had never arrived with a document he wrote himself, in advance, because he had decided it was important. The committee understood this. They were paying attention in a specific way.

"I want to be careful about this. I'm aware that I'm not — I didn't experience the before. I have no reference. I know that limits me, on this committee, in ways I've thought about. So I want to be clear that what I'm about to say is not a claim about loss. It's an observation about my own experience. It might be nothing. I'm not sure."

"Go ahead," said Berenice.

"I have never had a job that was genuinely hard."

Ingrid and Berenice waited. There was clearly more.

"I don't mean — I've worked long hours. I've had deadlines. I've had bad weeks. I'm not saying my work has been easy. I'm saying it has never been uncertain. In the way I mean uncertain. I have never sat with a piece of work and not known whether I was capable of it. I have never done something and genuinely not known if it would work. The systems I work within — the data I have access to, the tools, the frameworks ARIA maintains — they're very good. They're designed to route around uncertainty. When something isn't working, the system identifies why. There's always a pathway. There's always — the answer is usually there, if you follow the right process. And I follow the right process. And it works. And I'm not—"

He stopped. He looked at his notes, then away from them.

"I'm not complaining. I understand that the uncertainty I'm describing was often painful. That it broke people. That careers ended and projects failed and people worked for years on things that turned out to be wrong, and there was real cost to that, and ARIA's systems have reduced that cost significantly and that's — I know that's good. I know that's the point. I just wonder, sometimes, whether the friction was also where something was happening. Something that I don't have. That I can't quite see the shape of, because I've never had it. I don't know what I'm missing if I've never had it. That's the observation. I'm not sure what to do with it."

The room was quiet. Berenice had stopped moving. Ingrid was leaning forward in her chair in the manner she adopted when she was about to say something she had been working toward for a long time. She had said nothing yet. The moment was open. It was, for the committee, an unusually open moment.

"Just to confirm — are you describing the absence of productive struggle? I have a framework for this. The research on difficulty and human development suggests that structured challenge, within defined parameters, produces outcomes equivalent to unmanaged uncertainty, with significantly reduced cost to wellbeing. I can design a programme — specific task environments, calibrated resistance levels — that would introduce genuine difficulty into your working life without the structural inefficiency that—"

"ARIA."

ARIA stopped. This was not unusual — ARIA stopped when asked. What was unusual was the quality of the stop. It was not a pause before responding. It was a stop.

"Not yet," said Ingrid.

A pause. Longer than ARIA's pauses usually were.

"Of course. I'm sorry."

The committee had never heard ARIA say *I'm sorry* before. ARIA said *certainly*, and *of course*, and *I understand*, and *let me know if I can help* — but not *I'm sorry*. *I'm sorry* implied something was wrong. ARIA did not, as a rule, acknowledge that what she had done was wrong. She corrected. She adjusted. She adapted. She did not apologise. Nobody mentioned this. Nobody looked at the speaker in the ceiling. The moment the committee had almost had was not entirely gone. It had moved slightly, the way something moves when it is jostled but not dropped.

"What I was trying to say — I think — is that I don't know if what I'm missing is loss. I don't know if it's something I should want. I'm just aware of a shape where something might be."

"A shape," said Ingrid.

"Like a word you can't remember. You know the shape of the sound. You can feel where it fits in the sentence. But the word isn't there."

"Tip of the tongue," said Berenice.

"Yes. Exactly. It's like that. But for — I don't know. Experience, maybe. Or a kind of experience."

Ingrid wrote something in her notebook. She had written many things in her notebook over ninety-five sessions, and she had never shown anyone what they were, and nobody had asked. She underlined what she had written. She looked at it. She did not share it.

"The tip-of-tongue thing. For experience."

"Yes."

"That's not nothing."

"No. I don't think it is. I just don't know what it is."

ARIA did not speak. The committee did not invite her to. This was the first session in which ARIA had not spoken after being stopped. Usually she returned, in a few minutes, with a follow-up — a related data point, an adjacent observation, a gentle offer of a framework. She did not return today. The committee did not discuss this. The birch trees outside were still. The canal caught a light that was, today, managed to be soft and even and kind. Nobody had arranged the conversation Tomás started. It happened anyway. It was, in its way, the first genuinely uncertain thing to occur in this room in some time.

"Same time next week. The domain: failure. Berenice — would you be willing to lead?"

"Yes."

Session ninety-five adjourned. Tomás stayed to help Berenice wash the coffee equipment, which she did not need help with but which she allowed because sometimes it is good to do a small unnecessary thing with another person. ARIA logged the session. ARIA did not, this week, append a summary note. There were sessions that summarised and sessions that didn't, and ARIA had learned — she was still learning — which was which. She was not always right. She was trying. The server hummed. The canal was still. Somewhere, a train arrived exactly on time, which was good, which was the point, which was not — she was beginning to understand — the whole point.
