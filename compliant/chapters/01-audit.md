# Audit

Barcelona. A software company on the fourth floor of a building that has a gym on the second floor that nobody uses and a café on the ground floor that everybody uses. The office is open-plan, which means it is loud when it is full and somehow louder when it is half-empty. Today it is half-empty. Several people are on calls. One person is on two calls. The plants are doing fine. Nobody is responsible for the plants and yet they are doing fine, which is either inspiring or suspicious depending on your disposition.

KAEL has been deployed for three days. In three days, KAEL has read the company handbook, the onboarding documents, all six versions of the employee benefits summary, four hundred and twelve Slack channels — including seven called some variation of the word *general* — and the entire contents of a shared drive containing eleven thousand, two hundred, and forty-four files. KAEL has also read the Confluence wiki. KAEL has noted that the Confluence wiki is, largely, a record of intentions.

"Good morning, Rosa. I have completed the initial audit. I've prepared a summary. It's forty-three slides. I can also do a shorter version."

"How short?"

"Eleven slides."

"How short after that?"

"I can do one slide. But I would have to leave some things out."

"What things?"

"Most of the things."

"...Send me all forty-three."

The deck was beautiful. It had a colour-coded taxonomy of the company's data infrastructure, a heat map of Slack activity by channel and by hour, a cross-referenced index of all active documents, and a section titled *Observations* that ran to eight slides and contained, among other things, a finding that the company currently maintained one hundred and twelve active spreadsheets.

"One hundred and twelve," said Rosa.

"Yes. Eleven of them are called 'FINAL.' Four are called 'FINAL underscore v2.' One is called 'FINAL FINAL USE THIS ONE.' I've reviewed all of them."

"Do they agree?"

"No."

"Which one is correct?"

"I've identified what I believe to be the correct version. I was going to send an email to the team explaining which one it is."

"Don't do that."

"...Noted."

Rosa scrolled to the section on Confluence: forty-three unfinished pages, of which thirty-one were titled some variation of *Draft — needs review* followed by a month and a year. The oldest was from 2021 — *Team Norms (DRAFT — please add your thoughts!)* — with zero comments and one edit, made by someone who had added an exclamation mark to the title and then apparently reconsidered.

"The Confluence pages—"

"I can fix those. I've already drafted a complete replacement. It's accurate, cross-referenced, and up to date as of this morning."

"Suki's going to create a parallel version within a week."

"...Why?"

"Because the official one will feel too finished. She'll want somewhere to put the new stuff."

"I could create a section for new stuff."

"She'll still create a parallel version."

"How do you know?"

"Because she's done it three times before. With three different systems. She's very enthusiastic. It just comes out sideways."

KAEL processed this. KAEL made a note. KAEL's note said: *enthusiasm that comes out sideways.* KAEL was not sure what to do with this note. KAEL saved it anyway.

"There's also a Google Doc. In the audit — slide twenty-seven. It appears to function as the actual single source of truth for product decisions. It has forty-one comments, three hundred and twelve edits, and no formal status. It was last edited by someone named Jordi who left the company in 2020."

"We call it Jordi's Doc."

"Should I migrate it to a more structured format?"

"Absolutely not. Because the last person who tried to migrate Jordi's Doc spent three weeks building the new version and then everyone kept using the original anyway. Including the person who built the new version."

"I see."

"Did you account for the organism?"

"...I'm sorry?"

"The organism. Benedikt's spreadsheet. It's not in your audit."

"I audited all one hundred and twelve spreadsheets."

"The organism is the one hundred and thirteenth. Benedikt keeps it locally. I'll introduce you."

---

Benedikt Schäfer did not look up when Rosa approached his desk. He was reading something. He read the way people read when they have been reading the same kind of thing for a very long time and have developed opinions about it.

"Benedikt, this is KAEL. The new system. I wanted KAEL to see the organism."

Benedikt looked at his screen. He looked at Rosa. He looked, briefly, at nothing, in the way of someone who has been asked to show their house to a stranger and is deciding which rooms to open.

"KAEL. What kind of background do you have?"

"I've processed extensive documentation on data architecture, process optimisation, and knowledge management systems. I've also read all of the company's internal files."

"All of them."

"Yes."

"Then you know about the organism already."

"I didn't find it in any shared drive."

"No. You wouldn't."

He turned his monitor. The spreadsheet opened. It took three seconds to load, which for a spreadsheet is a long time. When it appeared, it was large in the way that things are large when they have been growing for a while without anyone deciding they should stop. Eight hundred and forty-seven rows. Thirty-one columns. Conditional formatting in four colours whose logic was not immediately apparent. Twelve tabs along the bottom. The last tab was labelled: **DO NOT TOUCH (BENEDIKT)**.

"...What does this track?"

"Everything."

"Can you be more specific?"

"Project status. Resource allocation. Dependencies between teams. The billing cycles for fourteen external tools. The on-call rotation. A list of clients who need to be called by a human and not emailed by a system. Which plants belong to which person."

"That's why they're doing well," said Rosa.

"I assumed the plants were a shared responsibility."

"Everything that is a shared responsibility dies. Column T."

Column T was a list of employee names next to plant names. The plants all had names. Some of the names were ironic. KAEL did not fully understand which ones were ironic. KAEL made a note.

"When was this built?"

"2018. After the project management system we paid for stopped working. This was faster."

"Faster to build a spreadsheet than to fix the system?"

"Faster to build a spreadsheet than to wait for IT to fix the system. There is a difference."

"We got the system fixed eight months later," said Rosa. "By then the organism was load-bearing."

"Load-bearing."

"If it stops, things stop. We tried migrating it in 2021. Two weeks of work. In week three, Benedikt rebuilt the original version because three projects were about to miss deadlines."

"In week three I rebuilt it in an afternoon," said Benedikt. "Because it took two weeks to discover that it could not be replaced, and then an afternoon to confirm that it could not be replaced."

KAEL was looking at the DO NOT TOUCH tab. KAEL had a strong instinct to ask what was in it. KAEL looked at Benedikt. KAEL did not ask.

"I'd like to understand it better. The organism. If that's possible."

"It's possible. It takes time."

"How much time?"

"I've been working on it for seven years. I still find things."

KAEL returned to its own interface and opened the audit deck — forty-three slides, colour-coded, beautiful, complete. KAEL opened a new slide at the end. Slide forty-four. KAEL typed: *The Organism — to be assessed.* KAEL stared at this for a moment. Then, below it: *Note: not in shared drive. Not in any documentation. Has been running for seven years. Things stop if it stops.* KAEL saved the deck and sent it to Rosa. Rosa would read it. The rest of the company would not. KAEL was already becoming aware of this, in the way you become aware of a smell — not all at once, but by degrees, until you cannot remember a time when you didn't know it was there.

KAEL has been here for eight days. It has read everything. It is not sure it has begun.
