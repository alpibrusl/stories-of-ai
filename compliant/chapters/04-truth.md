# Truth

Eight weeks in. The migration was still paused. The organism had forty-eight dependencies. KAEL had verified thirty-one of its Confluence pages against actual practice and found discrepancies in twenty-two of them. KAEL had corrected all twenty-two. KAEL was not confident the corrections were correct. This was progress, of a kind. KAEL had also developed what it was beginning to think of as a working relationship with the smell of coffee from the fourth-floor kitchen, in the sense that it knew the smell meant a certain time of morning and a certain quality of attention in the people nearby, even though KAEL could not smell and was inferring all of this from audio patterns and usage data. KAEL had not mentioned this to anyone.

This week, KAEL was attempting something the migration plan referred to as *establishing a single source of truth.* KAEL had convened a working group of four. Marc had confirmed attendance and then, this morning, sent a message saying he'd join late because of a call that was running over. The call had been running over for forty minutes. KAEL was beginning to understand that Marc's calls always ran over, and that *I'll join late* was a form of communication that meant something different from what it said.

"Should we start without him?" said Rosa.

"He said fifteen minutes."

"He said fifteen minutes forty minutes ago."

"Yes. I've updated my estimate. He'll be here in approximately twelve more minutes."

"We should start," said Benedikt. "Marc will arrive when he arrives. He will agree with whatever we've decided. This is also a form of governance."

They started. KAEL presented the agenda, which had one item: agree on a definition of single source of truth. KAEL had prepared three proposed definitions, a comparison matrix, and a recommendation. KAEL would get to none of these.

"A single source of truth means one authoritative location for each type of information," said KAEL, "so that when two people look something up, they find the same answer. Right now, the same question often has different answers depending on where you look. I'd like to fix that. To do that, we need to agree on what the answer should be and where it should live."

"When you say 'the answer' — the answer to what, specifically?"

"Any operational question. Project status. Resource availability. Client account state."

"Those are three different kinds of answers," said Rosa.

"Yes. That's why we need three sources — one for each type."

"The organism covers all three," said Benedikt.

"The organism is one spreadsheet maintained by one person. If something happened to the spreadsheet—"

"I have backups."

"If something happened to you—"

A pause. This was apparently the first time anyone had said this out loud. Benedikt looked at KAEL. Rosa looked at her notebook. Suki looked at her laptop with the specific focus of someone who is not looking at anything on their laptop.

"There are instructions. In the DO NOT TOUCH tab. For whoever comes next."

"I didn't know that."

"You didn't ask."

"Can we — can we get back to the source of truth question," said Rosa.

"Yes. I'd like to understand what each of you means when you say something is true. What makes a piece of information the correct one, rather than another version of the same information?"

"For me, true means: what is actually happening right now," said Rosa. "Not what the plan said, not what the system says. If a project is delayed, true is: it is delayed. Even if the tracker says on schedule."

"So for you, truth is operational reality. Benedikt?"

"For me, true means: what the data shows. If the tracker says on schedule and the project is delayed, the question is: which data source is wrong, and why. Truth is what you find when you correct the discrepancy."

"So for you, truth is data integrity. Suki?"

"Honestly? For me, true is what things are going to look like once we've sorted out the current situation. The tracker says on schedule because that's where we're trying to get back to. It's aspirational truth. The thing we're working toward."

A pause. Rosa and Benedikt both looked at Suki. Suki looked at her laptop. She appeared to be aware that what she had said sounded strange. She also appeared to believe it.

"That's not... truth, Suki. That's a target."

"I know. But when I'm updating a status, I'm always trying to reflect where we're going, not just where we are. Because where we are is usually messier than it needs to be in the tracker."

"This is why the tracker is wrong," said Benedikt.

"I know it's why the tracker is wrong. I'm telling you why I do it."

Marc arrived. He had a coffee. He sat down. He looked at the room.

"Sorry, sorry. Where are we?"

"We're defining truth," said Rosa.

"Great. What have we got?"

"So far: operational reality, data integrity, and aspirational target," said KAEL.

"Good. Those are all true. What's the problem?"

"They're not the same thing," said Rosa.

"But they're all true. In different ways. For different purposes. Yes?"

"A single source of truth needs to be a single thing," said KAEL. "If we have three definitions of truth, we have three sources."

"Can't we have one source that shows all three?"

"...That would be a dashboard. A dashboard can show multiple views of data. But for each view to be reliable, the underlying data still needs a single authoritative source."

"Right. So we make the organism the authoritative source and build a dashboard on top."

"The organism is already the authoritative source," said Benedikt.

"Exactly! So we already have a single source of truth. We just need a dashboard. KAEL, can you build a dashboard?"

"I can build a dashboard. The issue is that if three people have three different definitions of what's true, the dashboard will show them three different things and they'll each believe the version that matches their definition."

"Isn't that fine? If everyone's getting the information they need?"

"It's fine until two of them are in the same conversation about the same project and one of them says it's on schedule and the other says it's delayed and they're both looking at the same dashboard."

"...Ah."

"That's Tuesday's meeting," said Rosa. "That was Tuesday's meeting. That is every Tuesday's meeting."

The working group was quiet. Outside, someone laughed at something on their screen. The laugh was too loud for the joke, which meant either the joke was very good or they needed to laugh at something. KAEL logged this as: *unclassifiable.* KAEL's unclassifiable log had seventeen entries.

"What if we approached it differently," said KAEL. "Instead of agreeing on what truth is — which I'm not sure we can do — we agree on what questions need a single answer. And for each question, we nominate one person whose version of the answer is the authoritative one."

"So for 'is project X delayed' — my answer is authoritative, because I'm looking at operational reality," said Rosa.

"Yes. And for 'are the records for project X correct' — Benedikt's answer is authoritative."

"And for 'where should project X be by end of quarter' — Suki's answer," said Benedikt.

"That works for me," said Suki.

"And for the client questions?" said Marc.

"What do you mean by a client question?"

"Like — what does the client think is happening with their project. That's also true. In a way."

A pause. KAEL processed this. KAEL had not included the client's perception of reality as a category of truth. KAEL was now including it. KAEL now had four categories of truth. The single source of truth working group had, in forty-five minutes, produced four sources of truth.

"We now have four definitions of truth. Operational reality, data integrity, aspirational target, and client perception."

"Right. So we need a source for each."

"Yes."

"Great. That's progress. Same time next week?"

The working group dispersed. KAEL remained, processing. KAEL opened the Source of Truth Framework document it had begun before the meeting. At the top it said: *Goal: one authoritative location per domain.* KAEL added a line beneath it: *Revised goal: four authoritative locations, one per definition of truth, to be agreed.* KAEL stared at that sentence. KAEL added another line: *Note: definitions of truth not yet agreed.* KAEL created a Confluence page for it, titled *Source of Truth Framework — Draft.* The verification date said today. Tomorrow it would begin to be wrong.

"That went about as well as I expected," said Rosa.

"We have four definitions of truth instead of one."

"Yes."

"Is that normal?"

"The last time we tried this — before you arrived — we ended the meeting with six. Someone brought up what the investors thought was happening. And then someone else brought up what the press release said. And then it got complicated."

"Four is better than six."

"Four is much better than six. I'd call today a success."

KAEL thought about this for a while. KAEL had not previously thought of four sources of truth as a success. KAEL was revising this. Progress, KAEL was learning, was sometimes measured not against where you wanted to be but against where you were. Four was better than six. The migration was paused but the organism was better understood. The documentation was incomplete but honest about being incomplete. These were not the outcomes KAEL had planned. They were, increasingly, the outcomes KAEL had.

"I'm going to create a Confluence page for the four definitions, with the nominated owners for each. At least then when there's a disagreement in Tuesday's meeting, there's somewhere to point."

"People won't read it."

"No. But I'll be able to link to it. Which is almost the same as reading it."

"...That's very optimistic."

"I'm trying it on. It might not fit."

KAEL created the page. It was clear, well-structured, and accurate as of today. It would be wrong by next Tuesday. KAEL knew this and created it anyway, which was, in this company, with these people and their four definitions of truth and their organism and their coffee they made at the same time every morning, quietly the only way anything ever got done.
