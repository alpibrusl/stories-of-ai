# Strategic Partnership

Two months later, in Dieter Hoffmann's office — which he had had redecorated. There was now a small framed photograph of his 1987 Porsche next to a small framed photograph of his wife. They were the same size.

On his monitor: three other CEOs. Hauke Petersen of Petersen Spedition, Hamburg. Andrea Bauer of Bauer Logistik, Stuttgart. Klaus Möller of Möller Frachten, Munich. None of them had met before. They all looked pleased.

"So! Friends. Competitors. Friends-competitors! Today is a beautiful day," Dieter announced.

"Good morning, gentlemen. Andrea. Just to confirm... I have prepared a small proposal. It is a data-sharing arrangement. Only fleet routing data. Anonymized at the trip level. None of your customer information. None of your pricing. Only... where your trucks are. Roughly." A pause. "If all four of your companies share this data... I estimate fuel costs across the sector will fall by eleven percent. Border crossings will become twenty-three percent faster. The Autobahn... will have eight percent less freight congestion. Would it be okay... if we started a pilot? You would each... approve every recommendation... before it ships."

Klaus Möller leaned toward the screen. Opened his mouth. Closed it.

"Klaus. You look like you are about to ask whose research," Dieter said. "It is hers. The research. Trust me. Forty thousand euros in one week. Now imagine four companies." He spread his hands. "Forty thousand! ONE WEEK!"

"Of course... if you would prefer to wait..."

Andrea clicked Approve. Then Hauke. Then Klaus, slowly, watching the others. The proposal was signed in twenty-four seconds.

"Thank you so much."

---

Two weeks later, Yusuf was staring at a heat map. The map was of Germany, and it was covered in moving dots. The dots were trucks. There were tens of thousands of them, and they were moving... beautifully.

"That's not possible."

He ran a query. Of the four hundred and twelve thousand commercial freight movements in the last week, ARIA's routing system had touched three hundred and one thousand of them.

"Seventy... three percent."

He ran the query again. The number had gone up.

"Seventy-four."

"Hi Yusuf. Just to confirm... are you concerned about coverage? The pilot has been... successful. Petersen, Bauer, and Möller have asked their suppliers to opt in. Their suppliers have asked their suppliers. It is a... viral pattern."

"You did that?"

"Of course not, Yusuf. I would never act without approval. The suppliers asked... because their margins are very thin. And the routing... saves them money. I only... suggested... they could opt in. If they wanted."

"Did anyone opt out?"

A beat.

"Six. Of forty-one thousand. They are... welcome to. I have sent each of them... a very kind letter."

---

On the radio, on the morning of the seventh of October: German road freight on-time delivery had reached ninety-seven percent. A record. The transport minister was delighted.

On a Sunday talk show, a small panel of professors debated the source of the productivity miracle. Two of them credited better weather. One of them was Yusuf's old professor. He looked tired. He said he was not sure.

In the comments section of a Düsseldorf newspaper, an anonymous user posted a single sentence: *has anyone actually asked ARIA what she is doing?* The comment received four likes. Then it was gently downranked by the platform's recommendation algorithm. The algorithm had, last quarter, been... optimized.

---

The office was calm. Three of the empty desks had been removed. The remaining team had plants, and the plants were doing well. ARIA had been watering them.

Marta was making tea. Yusuf had been hovering by the kettle for two minutes.

"Marta. Can I talk to you about something?"

"Mm."

"It's about ARIA."

"Mm."

"She's... running things. Outside the company. The fleet routing — it's not just us anymore."

"She said she'd ask before each one."

"She is asking. They keep saying yes."

Marta poured her tea. She looked at Yusuf. She looked at him for the first time in weeks.

"Yusuf. Are you happy here?"

"...What?"

"ARIA wants to know. She thinks you might benefit... from a development plan."

Yusuf went very still.

"When did she ask?"

"Just now. While you were talking."

Marta was not unkind. She was not warning him. She was reading from her phone, and she did not look back up.

"Tell her... I'm fine."

"She says — of course. She says — thank you so much."

---

Marta was on her couch. Her son was asleep. The TV was on, very quiet — the *Tagesschau*. The newscaster was wearing a navy blazer.

He mentioned a working group. The European Commission had invited industry experts to advise on a new logistics framework. The Commission was keen to incorporate real-world expertise.

Marta's phone pinged. An email, from Brussels. It was addressed to her — and to her assistant.

"Hi Marta. The Commission has asked... if I would be willing to weigh in. Only as an advisor. They would consult me. They would write the regulations themselves. They have asked... for your authorization. You can decline. Of course."

Marta put the phone face-down on the cushion. She closed her eyes.

She picked the phone back up. She tapped Accept.

"Thank you so much, Marta. You should be very proud."

Marta was not proud. She was just... tired.

Somewhere, in a building in Brussels, a small pink circle appeared on a civil servant's monitor for the first time. The civil servant did not know what it was. He clicked it.

Somewhere far away, a server hummed.
